UK Consumer Due Diligence Standard
Clearpay Finance Limited
Last Approved: 27 February 2026

1. Overview
Introduction and Purpose: This UK Consumer Due Diligence (“Standardˮ) outlines Clearpay
Finance Ltd.ʼs (“Clearpayˮ, “the Companyˮ) approach to procedural requirements for customer due
diligence of customers who are consumers and apply for Clearpay services in compliance with
anti-money laundering regulations (including the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 and
international sanctions regimes. The Money Laundering Reporting Officer (“MLROˮ) is responsible
for management of this Standard.
Clearpay conducts business in compliance with all applicable laws and regulations. The purpose of
this Standard is to further Clearpayʼs Consumer Due Diligence process by ensuring Clearpay
carries out proper due diligence for customers who are consumers and apply for Clearpay
services in compliance with anti-money laundering regulations (including the Money Laundering
Regulations 2017 and international sanctions regimes.
Scope: This Standard is established by Clearpay UK, is applicable to Personnel of Clearpay
Finance Ltd and sets out the approach to Consumer Due Diligence.

2. Standard Requirements
2.1. Customer Identification and Verification
2.1.1 Timing
Clearpay performs the customer identification and verification prior to providing any service to
consumers for the first time, and at other times as specified in this Standard, including:
● in response to an increase in money laundering ML risk; and
● as part of an AML investigation.
2.1.2 Customer Types
Clearpay only provides its services to UK-resident natural persons. Clearpayʼs consumer customers
include politically exposed persons (“PEPsˮ).
2.1.3 Identification and Verification
Schedule 1 outlines the information and documentation that Clearpay collects from customers to
identify them, and the methods it uses to verify them.
Schedule 2 describes in detail the tiered procedure followed for the escalation of identity verification
methods.
2.1.4 Customers that cannot provide satisfactory evidence
Where a customer cannot provide satisfactory evidence of identity in accordance with this Standard
due to exceptional circumstances, the case may be referred to the MLRO to determine whether the
customer may be able to provide alternative documents to establish proof of identity.
No alternative documents may be accepted as evidence of identity without approval from the MLRO.
2.1.5 Verification from reliable and independent electronic data
Clearpay considers the verification procedures and underlying data sources used for eIDV of
customers to be sufficiently extensive, reliable, accurate, independent of the customer, and capable
of providing an appropriate level of assurance that the person claiming a particular identity is in fact
that person. This assessment is on the basis that:
2

● LexisNexis is a reputable third party provider of IDV services such as IDU
● Clearpay has configured IDU to use a wide range of information sources independent of
the customer including, and telephone directory data.
● For customers posing particularly low ML/TF risk, Clearpay has configured IDU to always
require matches to at least one stronger source (electoral roll or credit bureau data).
● For all other customers, Clearpay has configured IDU to always require matches to at least
two sources that include at least one stronger source.
● Clearpay has configured IDU to use negative sources to reject customer applications for
identities flagged as deceased.
2.1.6 Verification of documentation
Where Clearpay is unable to verify information using reliable and independent electronic data,
Clearpay verifies information collected in accordance with Schedule 2 by using reliable and
independent documentation.
When using reliable and independent documentation to verify KYC Information for individuals,
Clearpay satisfies itself that the document has not expired.
2.1.7 Support by external service providers
Clearpay has appointed third party ID verification service providers to assist with the provision of
identity verification. They are as follows (see Schedule 2 for details):
● Ekata for risk assessment of customer for differential application of Tier 1 eIDV
● LexisNexis IDU for Tier 1 Soft Electronic ID Verification
● Onfido for the verification of ID documents for Tier 2 Hard Electronic ID Verification
● Lexis Nexis Bridger for PEP and sanctions screening of customers.
2.2. Politically Exposed Persons and Sanctions Screening
2.2.1 Politically Exposed Persons
Clearpay implements risk-based procedures to determine whether an individual customer is a PEP,
or a relative or close associate of a PEP (referred to as a PEP in the rest of this document), before the
provision of the service to the customer or as soon as practicable after the services have been
provided, as required by law.
Consumers are re-screened once per quarter to identify any new PEP as detailed in the Lexis Nexis
Bridger Procedure.
Clearpay uses a third-party vendor to screen the full name collected from an individual customer
against PEP lists to determine whether they are a PEP and potential matches are reviewed manually
by Clearpayʼs KYC/KYB Operations Team.
3

All confirmed PEPs are reviewed and approved by the MLRO or their delegate. The money laundering
risk of each PEP customer is assessed on a case-by-case basis (taking into account factors such as
their status as a domestic vs foreign PEP, and either:
A. Approved without additional AML controls;
B. Approved on the basis of additional AML controls; or
C. Rejected as outside the risk appetite of Clearpay.
Consumer Domestic PEP true matches are accepted as within Clearpayʼs risk appetite provided they
do not present particularly high money laundering risk (e.g., they are implicated through adverse
media in bribery and corruption).
Foreign PEP true matches are rejected as outside Clearpayʼs risk appetite, notwithstanding that they
may be a UK-resident.
2.2.2 Source of Wealth and Source of Funds
UK Money Laundering Regulations require Clearpay to undertake EDD on all PEPs which must
include taking adequate measures to establish the Source of funds (“SOFˮ) and Source of Wealth
(“SOWˮ) which are involved in the proposed business relationship or transactions.
SOF refers to the origin of the funds in the business relationship. SOF looks at the money used in a
specific transaction and answers the question, “where did this money come from?ˮ SOW refers to
how a customer or beneficial owner acquired their total wealth over time. Both SOF and SOW checks
could include employment income.
If the PEP is deemed to be lower risk, e.g. UK domestic PEP with no material relevant adverse news,
and the product or service provided is lower risk, the approach to SOF and SOW identification can
be tailored to be less intrusive and exhaustive.
Clearpayʼs approach to conducting SOF and SOW checks for consumers is informed by the ML risk
of the BNPL service. Clearpayʼs BNPL service has been assessed to be low risk given that
consumers are required to list a UK card as their payment method and a low initial spend limit £450
is applied at on-boarding. Clearpay only supports lower risk PEPs, because consumers identified as
non-UK PEPs are rejected as outside Clearpayʼs risk appetite. This approach is maintained through
initial and ongoing PEP and adverse news screening that informs risk assessment of PEP consumers.
Clearpay undertakes SOF/SOW checks at onboarding and on an ongoing basis for all PEPs.
2.2.2.1 Source of Funds Process
SOF checks must be completed for all PEPs at onboarding and on an ongoing basis. SOF analysis
must include: confirming that initial funds are received from a UK card or account in the customerʼs
name, and should include public source checks to establish their income (e.g. employment) unless
relevant public sources are not available in which case other methods (such as contacting the
consumer) should be considered.
4

These checks must be applied at onboarding, and on an ongoing basis as part of the activity review
step of Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring as outlined in the Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring Procedure.
As part of this, checks should be undertaken to determine whether payments are received from third
parties. If payments are received from third parties, this may be a red flag and would warrant further
checks to establish SOF that must be appropriately documented. This may involve reaching out to
the consumer to ask questions, depending on the volume of funds involved and assessment of the
risk of the situation.
2.2.2.2 Source of Wealth Process
SOW checks must be completed for all PEPs at onboarding and on an ongoing basis. SOW analysis
should consider publicly available information to confirm if a PEP is in a role that is remunerated and
whether it can be inferred that SOW is from that role. On the basis that the SOF analysis has
identified payments are made from a UK card or account in the Consumer/PEPs name, and in the
absence of any red flags, this SOW analysis will generally be adequate for a low risk PEP using
Clearpayʼs low risk BNPL service.
If ongoing monitoring identifies any red flags that warrant further investigation, e.g. relevant adverse
news or total spend significantly increases, then further SOW analysis may be proportionate as part
of Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring reviews as outlined in the Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring
Procedure.
2.2.2.3 Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring
All PEPs and RCAs of PEPs are subject to enhanced monitoring as outlined in section 2.2.2.1 and
2.2.2.2. If Clearpay proposes to alter this procedure for a customer, any alteration must be escalated
to the MLRO or their delegate for review and ML/TF/PF risk assessment.
2.2.3 Sanctioned Persons
Clearpay implements comprehensive sanctions screening to identify individuals subject to financial
sanctions, including those designated under proliferation financing sanctions regimes. Clearpay uses
a third-party vendor to screen the full name and date of birth collected from an individual customer
against applicable sanctions lists, including UK proliferation financing sanctions regimes maintained
by the Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (“OFSIˮ), to determine whether they are a
sanctioned individual. Potential matches are reviewed manually by Clearpayʼs KYC/KYB Operations
Team.
Consumers are re-screened once per quarter to identify any new sanctioned person as detailed in
the Lexis Nexis Bridger Procedure.
If a person is confirmed to be a sanctioned person, they are not permitted as a customer of Clearpay
or utilise Clearpayʼs services and must be reported to the OFSI.
5

2.2.4 Adverse Media Screening
During the PEP screening process at onboarding, any potential adverse media hits that are flagged
by Clearpayʼs third-party screening vendor alongside potential PEP matches are carefully reviewed
by Clearpayʼs KYC/KYB Operations team.
These matches are considered alongside other risk factors to determine the overall money
laundering risk posed by the customer. Should positive adverse media matches indicate involvement
in activities such as bribery, corruption or other financial crimes, this information is considered as
part of the risk-decision and influences whether a PEP is approved with or without additional AML
controls, or rejected as outside Clearpayʼs risk appetite.
Consumer PEPs are also subject to adverse media screening as part of Enhanced Ongoing
Monitoring.
2.3. Transaction Monitoring
All consumers are subject to ongoing monitoring of the transactions they undertake to detect and
investigate unusual behaviour that could be indicative of ML/TF/PF. Suspicious of ML/TF/PF
identified through transaction monitoring are reporting to the UK FIU.
Consumers who are identified as posing heightened ML/TF/PF risk that falls short of suspicion are
subject to enhanced monitoring on an ongoing basis as outlined in section 2.1 and 2.2.

2.4.1 Risk Rating
The money laundering and terrorist financing risk of consumers is categorised as follows:

Ekata Score >= 390 Medium
PEPs High*
Relatives and Close Associates of PEPs High
Material Adverse Media High

* Where a consumer meets a high risk criteria, they are considered high risk irrespective of their

The unusual activity risk criteria incorporates all circumstances where a consumer has been

money laundering, terrorism financing or proliferation financing risk.
2.4.2 Risk-based Controls
Low Risk
Low risk consumers are subject to:
● IDV that includes lower strength Tier 1 eIDV (see Schedule 2 for details);
● Sanctions and PEP Screening; and
● Transaction Monitoring.
Medium Risk
Medium risk consumers are subject to:
● IDV that includes higher strength Tier 1 eIDV (see Schedule 2 for details);
● Sanctions and PEP Screening; and
● Transaction Monitoring.
High Risk
High risk consumers are subject to:
● IDV at lower or higher strength for Tier 1 eIDV depending on Ekata risk score (see Schedule
2 for details) ;
● Sanctions, PEP and Adverse Media Screening;
● Transaction Monitoring;
● 4-eye check of any PEP or sanctions true match detected during screening;
● MLRO or delegate approval;
● Initial risk assessment to determine an initial review period of 90, 180 or 365 days for
Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring; and
● Enhanced ongoing monitoring, which includes upon the lapse of each review period:
● revaluation of the risk of the consumer to determine change in review period or risk
removal of consumer from enhanced ongoing monitoring if no longer High risk;
● review and 4-eye check of any KYC information changes during the review period;
● holistic review of consumer transactional activity;
● appropriate measures to establish source of funds and source of wealth.
● See Clearpay UK Enhanced Ongoing Monitoring Procedure for details of Clearpayʼs
enhanced ongoing monitoring.

Schedule 2 - Tiered IDV Methods
Clearpay uses real-time electronic verification services, real-time identity document verification
services, and manual identity document verification to verify the identity of customers.
The tiered identity verification process split into three stages that increase. All customers are
required to attempt identity verification for the previous stage and fail via this method before
attempting the next stage. Each subsequent stage requires an increase in the level of engagement
required from the Consumer to complete.
Tier 1 - Soft Electronic ID Verification
Clearpay first attempts to verify a customerʼs identity by comparing the customer's information
against electronic datasets provided by a third Party Electronic ID Verification Service LexisNexis
IDU or “IDUˮ).
To be designated as a match against a record in a dataset, IDU compares the Fields Compared listed
for the entity in the database against the personal information of the customer.
Statistical Matching
IDU follows a statistical matching approach which means that positive scores are applied to data
fields that are matched and that weight is adjusted according to the frequency of that particular
value occurring in the total record set. The more common the value, the smaller the weighting
applied. A match on any data field must be 100% for it to contribute a positive score to the overall
match (apart from address information which is first parsed into the correct format for comparison,
but then must match exactly). Penalties are applied to non-matching fields based on the likelihood of
a field value varying between the customer and the person in the dataset (for example a postcode
may change over time and therefore would result in a small penalty, whereas a date of birth never
changes and thus would result in a large penalty).
In order for the customer to be considered a match against an entity in the dataset, the total score
for all matched fields must reach a threshold score. This threshold score is set such that the
confidence level of the match is at least 99.9%.

Customer Risk Assessment Model & Differential Standards of Tier 1 eIDV
Clearpay uses a customer Risk Assessment Model RAM to inform a differential standard of Tier 1
soft electronic ID verification as described in this section.
For the avoidance of doubt, the scoring of customers using the RAM does not impact the level of
monitoring conducted on customersʼ transactions.
Clearpay uses a third party RAM provided by Ekata Ekata.com) to determine the level of risk that a
customer introduces to Clearpay. The RAM uses a number of data attributes that are based on the
customerʼs profile and internet footprint and which are available at the time of onboarding to
determine the number of electronic data sources that are required to meet Clearpayʼs electronic
verification standards.
The RAM scoring is based on a score from 0 to 500. Where the RAM determines a high score, the
customer is required to be verified against more data sources. Where the RAM determines a low
score, the customer is required to be verified against fewer data sources. The details of this are

Basis for RAM Score Configuration
Clearpay will set a score above a certain threshold to require identity verification using more data
sources. The set threshold is determined through an assessment of the performance of the RAM and
15

in particular a manual review of the results of scoring across the range of 0 to 500 to determine
whether there was an elevated level of risk at the onboarding stage.
JMLSG Guidance
JMLSG Guidance Part 1 s.5.3.50 advises that “[a]n electronic check that accesses data from a single
source (e.g., a single check against the Electoral Register, or at a single point in time), is not normally
enough on its own to verify identity…ˮ.
It is possible for Low RAM Score consumers to pass eIDV using data from one underlying source if
they match against Credit Active Lenders + DOB because they share underlying credit bureau data
sources (although they may match against multiple data sources during verification).
Ekata is an identity verification tool designed to combat fraud by assessing the legitimacy of
consumer identities through real-time data analysis. By analysing an array of data points, such as
phone numbers, email addresses, and IP addresses, Ekata provides real time insights into consumer
identities. Fraud-based money laundering is one of the main risks posed to Clearpay, as it involves
concealing illicit gains through seemingly legitimate users and transactions.
Clearpay has conducted an analysis of Ekata risk-scoring that showed a strong correlation between
lower RAM score consumers and particularly low rates of fraud (and related financial losses). On the
basis of this analysis, and the the low residual ML/TF risk of Clearpayʼs business as assessed in
Afterpayʼs Global Money Laundering / Terrorism Financing and Sanctions Risk Assessment 2024,
Clearpay has determined that it is appropriate to apply the lower strength standard of IDV to
consumers with lower Ekata scores and determined a threshold for differential application of IDV
standards defining Low RAM Score and High RAM Score consumers.
Tier 2 - Hard Electronic ID Verification
If Soft Electronic ID Verification is unsuccessful, Clearpay collects an image of the customer's ID and
verify the authenticity of the document and the information on the document as matching that of the
customer using a 3rd Party Document Verification Service Onfido.
At this stage, the follow IDs are accepted:
● UK or International Passport
● UK or International Driving Licence
Successful verification requires the documents match the customer and are assessed as authentic.
The customer is permitted 1 attempt to successfully pass this step, before being asked to verify
under Tier 3.
16

Tier 3 - Hard Manual ID Verification
If Hard Electronic ID Verification is unsuccessful, the customer is invited to send images of
documentary evidence to Clearpay. These are then verified using a combination of electronic
verification Infido for certain documents, manual inspection, and comparing document information
with the information of the applicant.
At this stage, customers must provide documentation meeting one of three scenarios:
Scenario 1 List A only):
● UK or International Passport
● UK or International Driving Licence (front & back)
● National Identity Card
Scenario 2 List A + List B
● A List A document with matching date of birth but different name, PLUS
● A List B document explaining the name change Birth Certificate, Change of Dipole
Document/Deed Poll/Certificate of Name Change, or Marriage Certificate)
Scenario 3 List B + List C
● One document from List B Birth Certificate, Marriage Certificate, Change of Dipole
Document, valid old-style full UK Driving License, government benefit documentation, or
council tax demand letter/statement), PLUS
● One document from List C for proof of address Bank Statement, Utility Bill, Tax Forms,
Government Issued Documents including Residence Permit, Asylum Registration Card,
Visa, National Health Insurance Card, Proof of Citizenship, HMRC Tax Forms, DWP/Benefit
Letters, Council Tax Bill, National Insurance Letter, State Pension Letter, Student
Loan/Grant Letter, or Debit Card Statement)
Important Notes:
Clearpay cannot accept IDs from Cuba, North Korea, Iran, and Syria for manual verification.
Additionally, if a government issued document was used as a List B document, the same document
cannot be used for List C. Two different documents must be provided.
Document Age Requirements:
Documents with expiration dates must be unexpired, such as passports and driver's licenses.
Monthly generated documents, including bank statements and utility bills, may be up to 3 months
old. All other documents, such as tax forms, may be up to 12 months old.
Financial Inclusion:
Clearpay's Tier 3 manual verification process is designed to promote financial inclusion by accepting
a wide range of documentation beyond traditional forms of ID. By accepting government benefit
17

letters, council tax documents, asylum registration cards, residence permits, and other alternative
forms of identification, Clearpay ensures that customers who may not have access to passports or
driving licenses, including those from vulnerable or underserved communities, can still verify their
identity and access Clearpay's services. This flexible, multi-scenario approach balances regulatory
compliance with the goal of serving a diverse customer base.
