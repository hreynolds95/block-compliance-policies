1. Overview
Introduction and Purpose
This Compliance Quality Standard establishes a consistent framework for assessing and improving
the quality and effectiveness of compliance processes across Block, Inc. and its subsidiaries
(collectively, “Blockˮ). Block has designed its Quality Program to consist of two parts: Quality
Control (“QC”) and Quality Assurance (“QA”). The Program ensures that Compliance Operations,
with responsibility for implementing processes and procedures to meet Blockʼs BSA/AML and
Sanctions policy and program as it relates to areas such as Transaction Monitoring (“TM"),
Suspicious Activity Reporting (“SARˮ), Enhanced Due Diligence (“EDDˮ), and Know Your Customer
(“KYCˮ) reviews, meet regulatory obligations and internal quality benchmarks. The Quality Program
may be leveraged for other areas as designated, such as Appeals, Unacceptable Use and Terms of
Service Violation, within the scope of the Compliance Operations program.
Block is committed to maintaining a robust Quality Program and compliance with the laws and
regulations governing the conduct of its business. As part of this commitment, Block developed
this Standard to govern the Quality Program and define its objectives, principles, methodology, and
governance, ensuring independent, accurate, and transparent evaluation of compliance reviews to
strengthen Blockʼs control environment and promote continuous improvement. This Standard will
be supported by procedures and instruction; separated into specific control and assurance
components, or by function performing quality, where relevant.
A primary function of the Quality Program is evaluation of the effectiveness of the Compliance
operational functions in meeting the objectives and obligations under the BSA/AML and Sanctions
programs. The Quality Program primarily focuses on output and decisions related to alerts and
investigations, and provides a feedback loop for continuous improvement after a decision has been
made. The Quality Control QC function primarily focuses on real-time evaluation of decision
correctness either prior to a decision being made or shortly thereafter. The Quality Assurance QA
function) evaluates decisions made with a systematic review of existing workflows, processes,
procedures, training and where relevant, systems, to detect any weaknesses or inefficiencies that
1

could compromise quality, introduce errors that expose the compliance operations program to
gaps, or hinder effectiveness of the operations program meeting its obligations. Once identified,
these deficiencies are then thoroughly analyzed to understand their root causes, paving the way
for targeted remediation efforts. The goal of the Quality Program is to provide recommendations
and reporting that drive correction of issues as well as implementation of lasting solutions that
prevent recurrence and enhance overall operational procedures and program in fulfilling its
objectives under the BSA/AML and Sanctions programs.
Scope
This Standard applies globally to Block, Inc. entities and products, specifically those defined within
Covered Products, as applicable to Square, Square Financial Services (“SFSˮ), Cash App, Cash
App Invest (“CAIˮ) Afterpay and Clearpay, and covers all compliance-related reviews,
investigations, and quality control and assurance processes. In addition to application to Blockʼs
BSA/AML and Sanctions programs, the Quality Program may be leveraged for other areas as
designated, such as Appeals, Unacceptable Use and Terms of Service Violation, within the scope
of the Compliance Operations program.

2. Block, Inc. Requirements
2.1. Quality Principles
The Quality Program is built upon a set of foundational principles that ensure integrity, consistency,
and transparency in the evaluation of compliance processes. These principles define how Quality
operates across Compliance at Block, Inc. and its subsidiaries, providing a consistent framework
that promotes objectivity, reliability, and accountability in every review.
● Independence: QA reviewers operate independently from operational teams to ensure
objective and unbiased evaluations and QC reviewers are within operational teams
providing subject matter expertise. Oversight of QC by an independent QA function
reinforces the credibility and reliability of Quality results.
● Consistency: Reviews are conducted using standardized scoring criteria, definitions, and
procedures to ensure uniform application of quality measures across teams, products, and
regions.
● Coverage: Quality provides comprehensive oversight of all active Compliance queues,
utilizing sampling methods that cover BSA/AML and Sanctions operations programs.
Quality may also apply a risk-based approach allowing sampling adjustments based on
emerging risk or operational volume. Coverage decisions must be supported by
documented methodologies to ensure completeness and alignment with program priorities.
● Accuracy: Quality assessments validate the correctness, completeness, and rationale of
compliance decisions to ensure that control outcomes align with regulatory and internal
standards.
● Transparency: Quality findings are documented with clear rationales, evidence, and
recommendations to ensure that results are understandable and actionable by
stakeholders.
6

● Corrective action: Quality reviewers will issue a follow-up action to ensure any corrective
actions resulting from a Quality Review are addressed and remediated within a defined
Service Level Agreement (“SLAˮ), typically 7 days.
● Disputes: On receipt of a Quality Review, a Team Lead or approved DRI may file a dispute
on the quality review within a 7 day SLA of receipt, outlining the reasons for disagreement
with the review and scorecard. The Quality team will review the dispute and if necessary,
adjust the Quality Review and score. Any remaining corrective actions must be completed
by the agent within 7 days of the outcome of the dispute.
● Validation of corrective actions: the Quality team will review a selection of corrective
actions to ensure completion in full.
● Continuous Improvement: Quality insights are used to identify trends, inform training, and
drive process and system enhancements that strengthen compliance quality over time.
2.2. Methodology
The Quality methodology applies a structured approach to assessing compliance processes
through systematic sampling, scoring, and feedback. Reviews are conducted regularly and
encompass both quantitative and qualitative analysis.
Sampling approaches are risk-based and proportionate to the alert volume, product complexity,
and compliance risk profile. Quality reviewers evaluate accuracy, documentation quality, escalation
timeliness, and overall adherence to policy requirements.
Sampling methodologies incorporate stratified and statistically sound techniques, with detailed
requirements defined in Procedures. Sampling methodologies may be supported by automated
systems and tooling to improve consistency, efficiency, and auditability.
Sampling methodologies are subject to periodic review and validation to ensure continued
effectiveness, representativeness, and alignment with program risk. Quality reviews and
documentation must be captured using standardized templates and data fields defined in
Procedures to ensure consistency and auditability.

2.3. Scoring Framework
The Scoring Framework establishes the principles for evaluating the quality, accuracy, and
effectiveness of compliance activities. It ensures that Quality results are applied consistently
across all programs and entities while allowing flexibility for program-specific procedures.
Each Quality Program (e.g., Square and Cash App) maintains its own Procedures that define
scoring scales, thresholds, and attribute weightings based on the nature of the product, regulatory
requirements, and associated risks. These detailed frameworks specify how individual attributes
are scored and how overall quality ratings are calculated.
At the Standard level, scoring outcomes must:
8

● Be derived from a consistent and documented methodology.
● Include clear definitions for rating categories (e.g., Pass/Fail/Needs Improvement).
● Apply uniform principles for weighting, materiality, and review documentation.
● Allow comparability across time periods and business lines for trend analysis.
Quality Programs must ensure their scoring frameworks align with these overarching principles.
These frameworks should also be periodically calibrated to maintain consistency and objectivity.
To support consistency in reporting and trend analysis, Quality teams will also monitor key
quantitative indicators such as the overall Passing Rate, Average Score and Decision Accuracy.
● Passing Rate refers to the proportion of reviewed cases that meet or exceed the minimum
acceptable quality threshold, expressed as a percentage of total reviews completed within
a defined period. The minimum Passing rate is:
○ 90% minimum pass rate
● Average Score represents the mean quality score across all reviews conducted within a
given timeframe, used to assess overall Quality performance and directional trends. The
minimum Average score is:
○ 95% minimum average rate
● Decision Accuracy refers to specific questions within a quality review that specifically
address whether a decision was made correctly, expressed as a percentage of total
reviews completed within a defined period. The minimum Decision Accuracy score is:
○ 95% minimum accuracy rate
These metrics serve as standardized benchmarks across programs, providing leadership with
clear insights into quality performance without prescribing specific scoring models. Program-level
procedures will define the calculation methods, targets, and thresholds for these indicators.
2.4 Management Reporting
On an ongoing basis, the Quality Standard Owner and/or their designee monitors Quality outcomes
and reports monthly to Compliance Leadership. Feedback loops ensure that process owners
implement corrective actions within agreed timelines and provide updates on remediation
progress.
The Quality team will also publish detailed reports containing metrics and insights, with
recommended actions for any areas that are not meeting performance metric expectations on a
routine basis. The recommended actions will be provided to Operations Program Managers and
Compliance Training teams on a monthly basis as part of the Quality Feedback Forum for
alignment and completion.
9

Quality validates the implementation and effectiveness of corrective actions through defined
follow-up activities documented in Procedures. Quality Reviews, findings and remediation actions
are tracked through standardized systems or tools to ensure timely resolution, closure, complete
visibility and auditability.
Quality findings that indicate material risk, systemic issues, or repeated non-compliance must be
escalated in accordance with documented escalation procedures defined in Procedures.
Quality leverages analytical frameworks and multi-period trend analysis to assess control
effectiveness, identify systemic issues, and evaluate overall program health, with insights
incorporated into governance reporting and continuous improvement initiatives.
Detailed Quality reporting metrics, definitions, calculation methodologies, and governance
expectations—including quality performance indicators, service level performance, trend analysis,
remediation tracking, and training insights—are documented in Appendix 1 Reporting
Requirements) and supporting Quality Procedures.
2.5 Sustainability of the Quality Program
The sustainability of the Quality Program is supported through ongoing monitoring, periodic
calibration, and independent oversight of Quality activities. In addition to the annual review of this
Standard, Quality Reviews are subject to secondary review by senior Quality team members,
known internally as “QA over QCˮ and “QA on QAˮ reviews, to validate consistent application of
approved standards, procedures, and guidance. Program sustainability is further assessed through
recurring metric reporting, trend analysis, and governance oversight to ensure changes remain
effective over time.
All Quality-related documents, including review notes, scorecards, and feedback forms, must be
retained in accordance with Blockʼs Data Classification and Record Retention Policy and applicable
regulatory requirements. The Quality Program leverages Notary as its case management to store
review notes, scorecards, feedback forums, disputes, and related documentation. Performance
metrics are presented in Tableau.
3. Entity-Specific Requirements
3.1 Application of Standard
While the Quality framework and principles outlined in this Standard apply across all Block, Inc.
entities, each brand and business unit (such as Square, SFS, Cash App, Afterpay and Clearpay)
may tailor specific processes and procedures to reflect their unique operational procedures,
regulatory obligations, and product risk profiles.
Each Quality Program must align with the overarching framework described in this Standard,
including its principles, methodology, reporting expectations, and governance requirements.
10

However, flexibility is permitted to accommodate jurisdictional requirements, risk-based priorities,
and resource structures. Quality procedures are updated as necessary to reflect changes to
operational review processes, with Quality participating in relevant training to ensure continued
alignment without creating direct dependencies on operational standards.
Each entityʼs Quality Procedures must therefore:
● Translate Global Standards into Local Application: Ensure the core Quality
principles—independence, consistency, accuracy, transparency, and continuous
improvement—are reflected in the entityʼs operational Quality process documentation.
● Define Entity-Specific Scoring and Sampling Methodologies: Establish quantitative and
qualitative scoring models and sampling logic suitable for the entityʼs process volumes,
product complexity, and regulatory obligations.
● Document Service Level Agreements SLAs Define and maintain clear SLA requirements
for Quality processes, including expected case handling timeframes, review turnaround
times, feedback delivery windows and completion timeframe of follow-up actions by
operation teams. SLAs must align with the Governance Quality standards while reflecting
the operational and regulatory expectations of each entity. These documented SLAs
ensure timely, consistent execution of Quality processes and support effective governance,
remediation tracking, and workflow management.
● Document Roles and Responsibilities: Clearly outline the roles of Quality reviewers,
managers, process owners, and compliance leadership at the program level.
● Implement Local Reporting Frameworks: Maintain Quality reporting cadences aligned to
entity governance structures. At a minimum, Quality results should be summarized monthly
for operational leadership and quarterly for enterprise-level oversight (e.g., the Compliance
Operations Forum).
● Address Jurisdictional Requirements: Ensure Quality Reviews capture compliance
obligations specific to the entityʼs regulatory environment—for example, AUSTRAC
Australia), FinCEN United States), or the NCA United Kingdom)—while preserving
alignment with global Quality principles.
● Maintain Calibration and Training Programs: Facilitate regular calibration sessions,
cross-program Quality benchmarking, and training to promote consistent application of
Quality expectations across geographies.
● Support Continuous Improvement: Use Quality outcomes to identify process
enhancements, system improvements, or procedural updates at both the local and
enterprise levels.

4. Responsibilities & Reporting
4.1. Roles and Responsibilities
Effective governance of the Quality Program relies on clear accountability and well-defined
responsibilities across all levels of Compliance. Each role within the structure contributes to
maintaining the integrity, transparency, and reliability of quality assessments. This section outlines
the core responsibilities for managing, executing, and supporting Quality activities throughout
Block. It ensures oversight, operational execution, and continuous improvement responsibilities are
clearly defined and consistently applied across all entities.
● Quality Lead/Program Owner:
○ The Quality Manager is responsible for providing strategic oversight and
governance for the Quality Program, ensuring alignment across entities. This
includes approving Quality standards, framework updates, and significant
initiatives, as well as ensuring sufficient resources and structure for independence
and effectiveness. The Quality manager also owns the Quality Program Standard
and associated documentation, reviews performance reports and emerging issues,
and leads Quality reporting to Compliance Leadership and Governance committees.
● Quality Team Leads:
○ The Quality Leadsʼ responsibilities involve adapting enterprise Quality requirements
to local contexts, leading a team of independent Quality reviewers, escalating
significant findings to the Quality Lead, and supporting cross-entity consistency in
methodology and documentation.
● Quality Reviewers/Analysts:
○ Quality reviewers are responsible for conducting case-level quality assurance
reviews using approved methodologies, documenting their findings accurately and
impartially, and identifying trends and root causes of quality issues. They also
participate in calibration and feedback sessions to ensure consistency and maintain
adherence to documentation and confidentiality requirements.
● Quality Program Manager:
○ The Quality Program Manager is responsible for designing and documenting Quality
procedures and systems that support case review, sampling, and data integrity.
They collaborate with Compliance Data Analytics, and Engineering teams to ensure
accurate and auditable Quality metrics, and manage project timelines and
deliverables while maintaining consistent Quality documentation.
● Quality Training & Process Improvement Manager:
○ The Quality Training & Process Improvement Manager is responsible for leading
calibration sessions, managing disputes, and tracking local Quality performance.
They analyze Quality findings to identify training and process optimization
opportunities, then collaborate with entity Quality and Operational leads to
implement and validate improvements. Additionally, they maintain training records
and evaluate training effectiveness.
● Internal Audit and Second-Line Oversight (inclusive of GCMT
○ Internal Audit and Second-Line Oversight plays a crucial role in ensuring the
effectiveness of Quality Programs. They leverage Quality results in broader
assurance activities, provide independent validation of program effectiveness, and
12

coordinate with Quality and Compliance Leadership to ensure comprehensive and
non-duplicative assurance coverage.
● Compliance Process Owners/ Operational Program Managers:
○ Compliance Process Owners/Operational Program Managers are responsible for
receiving and addressing Quality feedback and findings. They review Quality
results, coordinate with Quality and Process Owners to understand issues and root
causes, and ensure timely remediation through process enhancements or
procedural adjustments. They also provide updates on remediation progress and
confirm the closure of findings, participating in sessions and meetings to align
operational practices with Quality expectations.
● Compliance Operational Leads:
○ Compliance Operational Leads are responsible for implementing corrective and
preventive actions from Quality findings, addressing Quality feedback within agreed
timelines with supporting evidence, and participating in dispute resolution and
process enhancements. They also monitor performance indicators, support
continuous improvement efforts through training and coaching and ensure
Follow-Ups are completed within SLA.
● Compliance Training and Policy Teams:
○ Compliance Training and Policy Teams are responsible for integrating Quality
insights into training programs and policy updates, ensuring that operational
guidance and development materials reflect Quality-driven recommendations, and
collaborating with Quality to maintain alignment between procedural documents
and current standards.
● Compliance Program Owners/Leads:
○ Compliance Program Owners are responsible for reviewing and addressing relevant
Quality insights in policy updates, programmatic improvements and the oversight of
the implementation of their program either through direct teams or outsourced
functions.
● Compliance Data Analytics Team:
○ The Compliance Data Analytics Team is responsible for providing sound statistical
sampling methodology and validation. They maintain data accuracy in dashboards,
develop analytical models, and support reporting and continuous improvement
initiatives through quantitative validation and visualization. This allows the Quality
team to identify data-driven insights, analyze error trends, and identify risk
patterns. The team also collaborates with Compliance Engineering, Quality Leads
and Program Managers to enhance reporting automation and analytical depth.
● Compliance Engineering Team:
○ The Compliance Engineering Team is responsible for designing, developing, and
maintaining Quality systems for case review, sampling automation, and data
integrity. They collaborate with Quality and Data Analytics teams to ensure accurate
and auditable Quality metrics, support the automation of Quality workflows, and
ensure Quality tools adhere to data governance, security, and privacy
requirements. Additionally, they partner with Quality to implement system
enhancements that improve quality oversight and reviewer efficiency.

Appendix 1 Reporting Requirements
1.1 Purpose and Scope
This Appendix defines the Quality Reporting Framework used to support oversight, transparency,
and continuous improvement across Blockʼs compliance operations. The framework establishes
standardized reporting components, governance expectations, and core quality indicators that
enable leadership to assess control effectiveness, operational performance, and overall program
health.
This Appendix applies to all Quality programs operating under the Block Governance Quality
Assurance Standard and supports enterprise-level and entity-specific reporting requirements.
1.2 Objectives of Quality Reporting
Quality reporting is designed to:
● Provide consistent visibility into quality performance and control effectiveness
● Identify systemic issues, emerging risks, and recurring deficiencies
● Support timely remediation and accountability
● Inform programmatic improvements, training priorities, and process enhancements
● Enable data-driven decision-making through structured governance forums
1.3 Core Quality Reporting Components
Quality reporting includes, at a minimum, the following standardized components:
Quality Performance Metrics
● Passing Rate: Measures the proportion of Quality reviews meeting or exceeding minimum
quality expectations.
● Average Score: Provides directional insight into overall quality performance across review
periods.
● Attribute-level trends: Highlights recurring strengths and deficiencies across quality
dimensions.
● Decision accuracy score: Provides specific feedback on correctness of decisions made.
Service Level Performance
● Measurement of Quality case handling time against defined Service Level Agreements
SLAs.
● Identification of sustained delays, backlog risks, or capacity constraints impacting Quality
execution.
Trend and Root Cause Analysis
● Multi-period trend analysis across queues, products, and review types.

● Identification of systemic issues, control weaknesses, or process design gaps.
● Root cause insights to support sustainable remediation.
Remediation and Issue Tracking
● Status of open and closed Quality findings.
● Progress against corrective action timelines.
● Validation that remediation actions have been implemented and are effective.
Training and Capability Insights
● Identification of training gaps or recurring error drivers.
● Inputs to calibration focus areas and training curriculum updates.
● Measurement of quality improvement following targeted training initiatives.
1.4 Reporting Cadence and Governance
Quality reporting is produced on a defined cadence appropriate to the audience and governance
forum, including:
● Governance Quality Assurance reporting twice a month performance metrics and monthly
for insights Quality Feedback Forum presentation of Quality observations and
recommended actions to Operations Program Managers, Training and Operations Leads
● Operational and Compliance Leadership reporting monthly performance metrics and
insights
● Enterprise and governance committee reporting quarterly as required, including the
Compliance Operations Forum.
Reporting outputs are used to support escalation decisions, prioritization of remediation efforts,
and strategic program enhancements.
1.5 Governance and Oversight Expectations
Quality reporting operates under defined governance controls to ensure consistency, accuracy,
and appropriate use of insights. These include:
● Oversight by the Quality Standard Owner and Quality leadership
● Alignment with enterprise compliance governance forums
● Escalation of material risks, systemic issues, or sustained performance deficiencies
● Independent review and challenge through second-line and audit functions, as applicable
Ownership of Quality reporting metrics and key performance indicators KPIs resides with Quality
leadership, including the Quality Lead, who is responsible for maintaining, reviewing, and updating
relevant metrics to ensure continued alignment with program objectives and risk priorities. Quality
metrics and KPIs are reviewed through established governance forums and in collaboration with
Compliance Leadership, with refinements made as necessary to enhance relevance, insight, and
decision-making value.

1.6 Supporting Documentation
Detailed reporting templates, dashboards, calculation methodologies, thresholds, and data
definitions are maintained in supporting Quality Procedures and operational reporting
documentation. These materials define execution-level requirements and may be updated
periodically to reflect program evolution, regulatory expectations, or tooling enhancements.
