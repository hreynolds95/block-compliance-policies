1. Overview
Introduction and Purpose: The Block, Inc. (“Blockˮ) Service Level Agreement (“SLAˮ) and Backlog
Management Standard (“Standardˮ) outlines the strategy for adherence related to SLAs. The key
components of this strategy include planning and resourcing for sufficient staffing, and developing
remediation plans in the event there is variance to the planning and alerts age outside the
established SLA.
This Policy is established by Block, Inc. (“Blockˮ) and is applicable globally across all Block
business lines, entities, and jurisdictions. References to “staff members'' throughout this document
shall refer to all full-time employees, fixed-term, and contingent workers (such as BPO who
support the operations and/or subsidiaries covered by this Policy, and include interns, temporary
workers, consultants, vendors, third-party providers, and contractors.
Scope: This Standard applies to Block, Inc., its entities, and affiliates (collectively, “Blockˮ),
including all products.
Definitions:
Terminology Definition
Service Level A defined timeframe within which a unit of work must be completed. This
Agreement SLA refers to the allowed number of hours, calendar days, or business days to
complete a review of an alert, suspicious activity report SAR investigation,
or other unit of work (such as an Office of Foreign Assets Control OFAC
report or partner escalation). In some cases, such as remediations, the SLA
may be expressed as a specific calendar date rather than a number of days.
1

Out of SLA An alert or other unit of work (i.e. a SAR case) that has remained open past
(“OOSLAˮ) the standard SLA but has not yet reached Backlog status. The Out of SLA
(“OOSLAˮ) period spans from 1 day past the SLA deadline up to 2x the
standard SLA. For example, if the standard SLA is 60 days, an alert is
considered Out of SLA from day 61 through day 120. At day 121, the alert
moves to the Backlog.
Backlog An alert, SAR investigation, or other unit of work that has remained open
greater than 2x the standard SLA. For example, if the standard SLA is 60
days, an alert moves to the Backlog on day 121.
Internal SLA An internal SLA is determined by the Operations Lead as appropriate to the
risk and complexity and is typically reflected as the due date in case
management systems and metrics reporting. The SLA can range from 24
hours/1 day, 7 days, 30 days or 60 days. SLA may be measured in calendar
days or business days, depending on the workflow.
For certain workflows, including SSP-enabled alerts, internal SLA may
operate in multiple stages, including:
● Initial alert review SLA,
● Defined customer response waiting period, and
● Follow-up adjudication SLA once the case is re-queued for analyst
action.
Regulatory SLA A Regulatory SLA is an external deadline set by regulators, such as filing a
SAR within 30 days of determining suspicion or filing an OFAC report within
10 days. Regulatory SLAs are binary.
Alert An alert is generated notification in queues within case management systems
triggered by predefined rules or risk indicators that require review by
Compliance Operations to determine whether further action or escalation is
necessary.
Queues A queue is a designated workflow within a case management system where
alerts or cases are routed for review by a specific team based on predefined
risk criteria or business rules.
Alert queues generate alerts based on heuristics or machine learning
models, or a combination of both.
Escalate to SAR An Escalate to SAR action is taken when there is reason to investigate
unusual activity further by a dedicated investigations team. When an
Escalate to SAR action is taken and a case is generated for investigation, the
internal SLA starts.
SARs may also be referenced as Suspicious Transaction Report STR and
Suspicious Matter Report SMR and other regional variations.
SAR An investigation by a dedicated team that will review activity and determine
Investigation whether there is suspicious activity, triggering the need to make a regulatory
report. The regulatory filing SLA begins upon the start of a SAR investigation.
2

SAR Filing The filing of suspicious activity report with the required regulatory body. The
process is defined in separate program and procedure documents by region.
OFAC Report The filing of a report on a sanctioned customer or transaction. The process is
defined in the Global Sanctions Compliance Program document.
Square Secure A Square Secure Profile SSP is a request for information that is sent to a
Profile SSP merchant to obtain additional information or documentation needed to
complete review and adjudication during an investigation.
For workflows that use SSPs, the original alert review is considered complete
when the SSP is issued, provided issuance occurs within the applicable
queue SLA. Subsequent case handling, including seller response review or
no-response adjudication, is governed by separate workflow timing and
queue rules.
2. Block, Inc. Requirements
2.1. Capacity Management
2.1.1. Capacity Model
Block leverages a capacity model for the planning and resourcing of its staffing in order to ensure
there are sufficient resources, both internally and externally, to maintain adherence to SLAs. Greater
detail about the capacity model and process used is outlined in the
Capacity Model Manual Updates Runbook , which is owned by the Compliance Analytics CAX
team. The capacity model is updated on a quarterly basis in partnership with Operations Leads.
2.1.2. Variance Monitoring
Once forecasts for volumes are provided for the capacity model, CAX conducts the initial
forecasting. Following this, the Compliance Operations Program Management (“COPMˮ) team
socializes these forecasts with Operations Leads and Workforce Management (“WFMˮ) Lead. CAX
reviews actual v. forecasted alert volumes on a monthly basis.
After WFM reviews the forecasts, they confirm whether or not the forecasts are in line with their
expectations for the program for budget and staffing. This review includes:
● Any known issues, enhancements, or changes to the program related to existing staffing
capacity, alerts, or technical issues
● Variations from past volumes and their root causes
● Other factors that may affect volumes
3

Once the forecasts are finalized, COPM provides visibility to the Head of Compliance Operations or
their delegates. For special projects, WFM partners with Operations Leads.
Daily Monitoring and Remediation
The Operations Leads are responsible for monitoring daily alert volumes and adherence to SLAs with
self-serve dashboards. They report progress of resolving any alerts that are out of SLA or in
backlog, and they track, report, and document progress against remediation plans to the Head of
Compliance Operations. Operations Leads are responsible for ensuring appropriate progress,
tracking resource allocation, and overseeing progress against remediation plans.
2.1.3. SLA Adherence
Operations Leads review team adherence to SLAs and work allocation at least weekly through Case
Management Systems and SLA reporting. In general, inventory is worked in accordance with SLA
aging, with cases prioritized based on proximity to their deadline. However, in certain
circumstances—such as specialized projects, credible threats of harm, life-threatening situations, or
indicators of a missing child—inventory may be reprioritized based on elevated risk considerations to
ensure timely handling of higher-risk matters.
Where leveraged, the outsourcing vendor will also provide weekly reporting to Operations Leads on
adherence to SLAs, progress on any alerts out of SLA or in backlog and confirm sufficient external
staffing is allocated to the work to complete it within the expected timeframe. Managing SLA
adherence is a collaborative effort between WFM and Operations Leads, with no single owner;
however, ultimate responsibility for adherence to SLAs remains with Block.
2.1.4. Variations
If variations present a risk of volume beyond the capacity of the existing staffing (internal or
external) and the potential for alerts to fall out of SLA and/or into backlog, an out-of-cycle capacity
request is triggered. In these instances, the Compliance Operations Program Manager COPM, in
collaboration with Operations Leads, drafts documentation outlining the increased volume and
corresponding capacity needs.
This request is then escalated to the Workforce Management WFM team to secure additional
staffing support (internal, external, or both), while also providing visibility to the Head of Compliance
Operations.
Out-of-cycle capacity requests are raised ad-hoc via the
Compliance Capacity Upcoming Initiatives Tracker and/or high priority tickets filed via
go/COPMintake for WFM. a. This recurring governance forum enables Compliance leaders to assess
operational metrics, trends, SLA and backlog risks, and overall program health, and to determine
necessary changes or remediation actions across Compliance Operations.
4

2.1.5. Out of SLA and Backlogs
Remediation Plans for Out-of-SLA or Backlog Alerts
When alerts are out of SLA or in backlog, Operations Leads work with WFM to develop and enact
remediation plans. The Operations Leads, a Compliance Operations Program Manager, and/or their
delegates partner on plan development.
Remediation plans include:
● Timeline to resolve out-of-SLA or backlog alerts
● Strategy to manage incoming alerts and prevent backlog growth
● Staffing timeline and resource allocation
● Priority ranking of out-of-SLA areas based on program risk
● Downstream impacts (e.g., SAR escalations, investigation timelines, regulatory SLAs)
Operations Leads Responsibilities:
● Share mitigation steps to maintain SLA adherence
● Report staffing limitations or concerns
● Assist in preparing out-of-cycle capacity reviews (as noted above)
● Ensure additional staff (internal or external) are properly trained
● Implement extended review periods back to the alert date
● Provide sufficient oversight for quality, completeness, and procedural adherence
Monitoring and Reporting
Operations Leads monitor progress against remediation plans and report at least weekly on:
● Progress made
● Changes to expected resolution timelines
● Other relevant factors
● Continued monitoring of the capacity model, actual alert volumes, and monthly metrics
reviews occurs to minimize additional program risk
Documentation:
● All documentation produced during remediation is retained for management, audits, or
examinations.
2.1.6. Missed Regulatory SLA
Regulatory SLAs related to SAR filings are monitored through formal tracking mechanisms
maintained by Compliance Operations. These controls include, but are not limited to:
● Ongoing tracking of SAR case filing deadlines;
● Automated alerts and aging reports identifying cases approaching regulatory due dates;
● Periodic management review of timeliness metrics to ensure adherence to regulatory
obligations.
5

This monitoring framework is designed to ensure compliance with applicable filing requirements,
including FinCENʼs 30-calendar-day filing deadline from the date of initial detection of facts that may
constitute a basis for reporting.
Regulatory Breach Management
A regulatory breach occurs when a SAR filing is completed after the applicable regulatory due date.
In the event of a confirmed regulatory SLA breach, the Operations Leads or their delegates are
responsible for ensuring the following actions are taken:
1. Immediate Remediation
○ Confirm that the SAR is filed without further delay.
2. Documentation and Recordkeeping
○ Record the breach in the designated regulatory breach repository (e.g., Human
Error or System Issue tracker).
○ Complete the required breach documentation template for regulatory SAR
breaches, including:
■ Date due vs. date filed
■ Root cause analysis
■ Impact assessment
■ Corrective and preventive actions
3. Root Cause Evaluation and Corrective Action
○ Assess whether the breach resulted from human error, process gaps, capacity
constraints, or system issues.
○ Implement appropriate remediation measures to address the underlying cause and
prevent recurrence.
4. Escalation and Governance Reporting
○ Escalate the breach to applicable Compliance leadership.
○ Include late filing metrics and root cause summaries in monthly operational reviews.
○ Report aggregate late SAR filing statistics and drivers in quarterly Board reporting,
as applicable.
The same escalation, documentation, and corrective action standards apply to any other missed
regulatory SLA to ensure consistent governance, transparency, and audit defensibility.
2.2. Queue Management
2.2.1. Queue Change Governance
Queue changes are governed by multiple cross-functional teams. The COPM team is responsible for
coordinating capacity planning, socializing volume forecasts, and partnering with Operations Leads
on remediation efforts. Operations Leads are responsible for ensuring their teams can absorb
expected volume increases and adjusting implementation timing based on available capacity.
6

The Model Risk Management MRM team within Compliance Engineering & Technology CET owns
the Block - Model Change Management Procedure (go/cmcm-process) , which governs how
changes to detection models and queues are proposed, analyzed, approved, implemented, and
documented. Any new queue or logic change impacting volume requires a Compliance Model
Change Management/Rule Change Request via JIRA.
Compliance Operations uses the Detections Requirement Document DRD Template to document,
assess, and approve the creation, modification, or deprecation of detection rules and models, and
submits the completed DRD via the Detections DRD Intake Form.
2.2.2. Prioritization of Queues
Operations Leads may organize staff based on alert volume and internal team priority of queues
based on risk, importance to program and other factors relevant to prioritizing alerts and
investigations. Operations Leads may rotate the focus in order to keep adherence to SLAs and
reduce risk in their programs.
2.2.3. Determination of Internal SLAs
The Head of Compliance Operations will collaborate with Operations Leads or their delegates to
determine SLAs appropriate to a queue during the design phase and with reference to applicable
Regulatory SLAs. The SLA is implemented with consideration for risk, regulatory requirements,
industry norms, importance of the queue to the BSA/AML program and any other relevant
considerations. Internal SLAs for initial cases range from 24 hours, 1 business day, 7 days, 15 days,
30 days and 60 days.
For certain workflows, including SSP-enabled alerts, internal SLAs may operate in multiple stages,
including:
● Initial alert review SLA,
● Defined customer response waiting period, and
● Follow-up adjudication SLA once the case is re-queued for analyst action.
2.2.4. Validation of Queue Forecasts
When the capacity model provides forecasts for volumes, a Compliance Operations Program
Manager socializes the volumes with Operations Leads and WFM, and provides visibility to the Head
of Compliance Operations or their delegate.
CAX validates forecasts internally, reviewing variations to past volume, root causes, and other known
factors that can affect volumes in order to provide a comprehensive forecast for budget and staffing.
The CAX team has dedicated members assigned to each vertical (program) to review forecasts in
partnership with Ops Leads. To determine which CAX teammate supports a specific program, refer
to the Risk & Compliance Operations Data Support . When Operations Leads or WFM become
7

aware of any forecast-impacting information, they reach out to the corresponding vertical lead on
the CAX team.
Where queues are implemented for new areas of monitoring and lack historical data to forecast
effectively, queues may be launched in a pilot or sample phase to evaluate volumes in line with
expectations and to achieve the right balance of productivity, coverage, and procedures before they
are moved into production.
2.2.5. Optimization of Queues and Tuning
Optimization of queues and related tuning activities are performed to maintain robust detection
performance in line with approved modeling guidelines. This includes reducing false positives,
managing manual review volumes, and ensuring case management systems and other tooling enable
staff to complete investigations accurately and efficiently.
The Model Risk Management MRM team monitors model and queue performance and conducts
pre‑implementation and final validation of changes to queuing logic. Queue owners initiate proposed
changes through the Compliance Model Change Management CMCM process (go/cmcm-request).
In-scope changes to queue logic must be approved by the designated Compliance Program
Approver(s) and the Operations Approver (when alert volume is expected to change)—identified in
the CMCM Approval Matrix (go/cmcm-approvers).
3. Compliance & Enforcement
3.1. Roles and Responsibilities:
● Operations Lead:
○ Day-to-day queue management (i.e. responsible for a set number of queues to their
team), assignment of work or rotations to complete work and review of SLA
adherence.
○ Operations Lead can be used to also make reference to a KYC Manager,
Compliance Manager, Lead or variations of title that indicate they are responsible
for a team that performs work requiring SLA adherence.
● Heads of Compliance Operations:
○ Responsibilities include oversight of queue management, assignment of work and
adherence to SLAs by the Operations Leads; responsible for capacity planning.
○ The Head of Compliance Operations is the lead for Operations Leads for KYC,
Sanctions, Investigations, and SAR. Additional responsibilities include reviewing
updates or changes to queues and development of new queues to ensure queues
are fit for their purpose, added to the capacity model and sufficient resourcing is
available and allocated, the SLA is appropriate to the queue and relevant
procedures, training and user guides are developed.
● Compliance Operations Program Management COPM
8

○ The COPM team is responsible for coordinating capacity planning, socializing
volume forecasts, drafting capacity documentation, and partnering with Operations
Leads on remediation efforts.
○ The COPM team architects and drives operational excellence through standardized
processes. It enables transformation by rapidly implementing technology solutions
and capabilities.
● Head of Compliance Business Unit:
○ Oversight of sufficient resourcing and recommends to Compliance Core approval of
resources, oversees SLA performance, metrics and capacity model forecasts and
budget.
○ Head of Compliance Business Unit references either the Compliance Core member
(such as Head of Square Compliance; Head of Cash App Compliance), the local
Compliance Country Lead or a delegate.
● Compliance Core:
○ Approves resourcing for staffing (internal) or budget (external staffing) and
receives reporting on adherence to SLAs performance.
● Compliance Analytics CAX
○ Owner of the capacity models, SLA dashboards, and any metrics delivered in
metrics meetings.
○ Responsible for all forecast data, which is derived from inputs provided by
Operations
○ Maintains and manages forecast adjustments via Google Sheets (current state),
including:
■ Manual overrides
■ Percentage adjustments
■ Efficiency initiative benefits
■ Off-grid work
○ Primary point of contact for any information or updates that may impact volume
estimates—please communicate changes proactively to ensure forecast accuracy
● Compliance Strategic Operations:
○ Approval of resources and budgets.
● Outsourcing Program Managers:
○ Responsible for engagement of outsourcing vendors, contracts to perform work
and available resourcing.
○ Outsourcing vendors are providers of external staffing, partners that assist in the
adherence to SLAs, remediate out of SLA alerts or remediate backlogs as directed.
The Operations Leads and Head of Compliance Operations are ultimately
responsible for the outsourcing vendors adherence to SLAs and timely resolution of
out of SLA alerts and backlog remediation.
● Compliance Data & Machine Learning Team:
○ Data and Machine Learning ML teams develop monitoring queues that generate
alerts and add SLA into case management systems.
○ The Data and ML teams also forecast volumes of alerts and SAR investigations that
are ingested by the capacity model and used to plan resourcing.
● Workforce Management WFM Lead:
The WFM Lead is responsible for ensuring that sufficient staffing capacity is operationally
9

available to meet SLA requirements across supported queues. This includes securing,
allocating, and maintaining appropriate internal and external staffing levels in alignment
with forecasted volumes and operational priorities. The WFM Lead will be responsible for
ongoing workforce planning and execution across weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annual
planning cycles, ensuring alignment between forecasted demand, staffing capacity, and
SLA requirements
Responsibilities include:
● Partnering with Operations Leads and Compliance Analytics CAX to translate
volume forecasts into staffing plans
● Ensuring staffing is secured, scheduled, and deployed in accordance with
capacity plans and emerging operational needs
● Coordinating the onboarding, readiness, and ramp-up of internal and outsourced
staff to support queue demands
● Maintaining oversight of workforce coverage to ensure resources are aligned to
priority queues as directed by Operations Leads
● Monitoring workforce execution to ensure staff are:
○ Actively working assigned inventory
○ Adhering to prioritization frameworks defined by Operations Leads
○ Meeting productivity and throughput expectations within their scope of
work
● Escalating staffing gaps, coverage risks, or performance concerns that may impact
SLA adherence
While the WFM Lead is responsible for workforce readiness, deployment, and execution
oversight, ultimate accountability for SLA adherence and queue performance remains
with Operations Leads and the Head of Compliance Operations.

On an ongoing basis, the Standard Owner and/or their designee monitors activities for compliance

Reporting may cover specific information related to:
● Policies and standards
● Procedures
● Audit/exam findings
● Issues management
● Training
● Complaints

3.4. Exceptions
The Standard Owner or designee will have authority to approve exceptions and/or exemptions to this
Policy, provided that the proposed exception or exemption does not violate applicable laws and
regulations. Exception requests must be documented in writing, include a reason for a request, and
provide a description of the controls that the Company will implement to mitigate risks associated
with the exception. Any approved exceptions must be documented in writing and retained with
recordkeeping requirements. Any approved exceptions or exemptions should be submitted to the
Policy Governance Team. The Document Owner and/or designee will inform Compliance Leadership
and applicable committees, as needed, of exceptions.
