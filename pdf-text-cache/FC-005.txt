1. Overview
Introduction and Purpose:
This Afterpay Canada Limited (“Afterpay Canadaˮ, “Afterpay CAˮ) Anti-Money Laundering Program
(“Programˮ) outlines Afterpay Canadaʼs approach to anti-money laundering, counter-terrorism
financing, and sanctions compliance. The purpose of this Program is to establish comprehensive
guidelines governing Afterpay Canada's operations to identify, mitigate, and manage money
laundering and terrorism financing risks, while ensuring consistent operations and maintaining
regulatory compliance.
Afterpay Canada Limited, a British Columbia incorporated entity, offers "buy now, pay later"
installment products in Canada. As a subsidiary of Afterpay Australia Pty., Afterpay Canada
maintains this Program to meet subsidiary obligations under Australian AUSTRAC requirements,
partner expectations from banking and financial institutions, and to implement voluntary best
practices aligned with Canadian AML/CTF frameworks.
Scope: This Program applies to Afterpay Canada Limited, its employees, contractors, and agents
engaged by Afterpay Canada, or acting on Afterpay Canada's behalf, for the purpose of
performing services for the benefit of Afterpay Canada.

2. Block, Inc. Requirements
2.1. Parent Company Obligations
As a subsidiary of Block, Inc., Afterpay Canada operates within Block's global compliance
framework while maintaining entity-specific requirements. This Program aligns with Block's
Anti-Money Laundering & Counter-Terrorist Financing Policy and incorporates applicable global
standards adapted for Canadian operations.
2.2. Cross-Border Regulatory Framework
Afterpay Canada maintains dual compliance obligations:
Australian AUSTRAC Requirements: As a subsidiary of Afterpay Australia, Afterpay Canada
complies with applicable AUSTRAC obligations for foreign subsidiaries, including maintaining this
AML/CTF program and reporting requirements.
Canadian Regulatory Framework: While Afterpay Canada is regulated by the Bank of Canada
under the Retail Payment Activities Act RPAA for providing payment services, this Program
implements voluntary AML/CTF best practices aligned with Canadian frameworks and partner
expectations.
1 Though Afterpay Canada is not directly regulated by FINTRAC, nor subject to the PC Act, this Program highlights the

Afterpay Canadaʼs MLRO or their delegate will submit voluntary STRs to FINTRAC where warranted.

2.3. AML/CTF Legal Framework
In Canada, the anti-money laundering/counter terrorism financing ("AML/CTF") regime is set out in
the Proceeds of Crime Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act ("PC Act") and certain
implementing regulations. While Afterpay Canada is not subject to the requirements of, or a
reporting entity regulated under the PC Act, Afterpay Canada is nonetheless committed to
identifying, mitigating and managing indicators of ML/TF, sanctions evasion or other criminal
activity as a best practice. In this regard, Afterpay Canada undertakes certain KYC, risk
assessment and related procedures in order to ensure that Afterpay Canada does not establish a
relationship with or transact with individuals or entities that could be linked to money laundering,
terrorist activities, sanctions evasion or that can use Afterpay Canada to facilitate such activities.
As a subsidiary of an Australian entity, Afterpay Canada may also develop an AML/CTF policy
relevant to any obligations under Australian law.
As a British Columbia incorporated entity, Afterpay Canada is subject to compliance requirements
under the Canadian sanctions laws. To this end, Afterpay Canada maintains this Program which is
designed to ensure that it complies with and does not directly or indirectly assist others to
contravene such laws.
3. Afterpay Canada-Specific Requirements
3.1. Governance and Oversight
3.1.1 Board Approval
This Program is to be approved and adopted by the Afterpay Canada Board.
This Program should be endorsed for approval by the Canada Anti-Money Laundering Compliance
Officer prior to being presented to the Board.
3.1.2 Compliance Officer
Afterpay Canada will appoint an Anti-Money Laundering Compliance Officer, who is referred to as
the Money Laundering Reporting Officer (“MLROˮ), whose appointment must be approved by
Senior Management. The MLRO will be appropriately knowledgeable in AML-related matters, and
will understand Afterpay Canadaʼs products, services, Customers and geographies services and
the potential risks associated with such products, services, Customers and geographies.
The MLRO is responsible for implementing this Program, overseeing Afterpay Canadaʼs AML
Program, and ensuring regulatory compliance. As necessary, the MLRO may also serve as a liaison
between Afterpay Canada and law enforcement officials, government regulators, internal or
external auditors, service providers, and others regarding issues related to Afterpay Canadaʼs AML

Program, and reviewing and updating Afterpay Canadaʼs AML Program consistent with any new
statutes, regulations, or regulatory and industry guidance.
The MLRO will provide periodic reports to Senior Management, as needed, at least on a biannual
basis. Such reports may include information about any instances of non-compliance with this
Program, any feedback received from external stakeholders including any regulatory bodies, the
results of any review conducted, and any legal or regulatory developments bearing on the AML
Program.
The MLRO may delegate duties to other responsible Employees as appropriate.
3.1.3 Independent Review
This AML Program may be independently reviewed every two 2 years. The Independent Review
will be performed by suitably qualified internal or external personnel. The purpose of this review is
to assess the effectiveness of the AML Program design, whether it has been effectively
implemented and whether Afterpay Canada is complying with it. The Independent Review will
focus on either all or parts of the AML Program, using a risk based approach.
The results of the Independent Review will be provided to the Afterpay Canada Board.

3.3 Customer Due Diligence
3.3.1 Merchant Know Your Customer KYC
Before a Merchant is permitted to use Afterpay Canadaʼs services, Afterpay Canada will identify
and verify the identity of the Merchant and beneficial owners of Merchants such that Afterpay
Canada has a reasonable belief that it knows the true identity of the Merchant and beneficial
owners (“Merchant KYCˮ).
A ‘beneficial ownerʼ for the purpose of this Program means individuals (i.e., natural persons) who:
● Ultimately own (directly or indirectly) 25% or more of a Merchant; or
● Ultimately control (directly or indirectly) a Merchant.
Control includes control as a result of trusts, agreements, arrangements, understandings and
practices, whether or not having legal or equitable rights, and extends to exercising control
through the capacity to determine decisions about financial and operating policies. The types of
individuals that could ‘controlʼ are discussed in further detail below.
In determining whether to offer Afterpay services to Merchants in certain verticals, in addition to
the considerations outlined in this Program, Afterpay Canada has established a Global Merchant
Category Policy to determine where services can be offered to a Merchant and whether additional
risk based checks or controls may be appropriate.
Merchant onboarding also includes ongoing risk assessment through the Merchant Risk Rating
framework.
Merchant Risk Rating MRR Framework:
● Activation MRR (aMRR): Rule-based assessment during onboarding to identify high-risk
merchants requiring Enhanced Customer Due Diligence
● Continuous MRR (cMRR): Ongoing attribute-based risk assessment for existing merchant
Enhanced Due Diligence: High-risk merchants undergo additional verification and ongoing
monitoring as determined by the MRR framework.
Enhanced Beneficial Ownership Requirements
For high-risk corporations incorporated under the Canada Business Corporations Act, Afterpay
Canada consults Corporations Canada's database and reports material discrepancies within
required timeframes. This enhanced transparency measure supports improved beneficial
ownership verification.

Merchant Bank Details
For each Merchant, Afterpay Canada requires bank account information (routing number and
account number) for a chequing account in the name of the Merchant. A Bank Account
Assessment is conducted to confirm that a Canadian bank account titled to the entity or beneficial
owner is provided during the onboarding process. Common Canadian banks include:
● Bank of Montreal;
● Bank of Nova Scotia Scotiabank);
● Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce CIBC;
● Royal Bank of Canada RBC;
● Toronto-Dominion Bank TD;
● National Bank of Canada;
● Desjardins Group;
● Laurentian Bank of Canada; and
● Canadian Western Bank.
Afterpay Canada deems Merchants who use these Canadian banks to be low risk and therefore
does not require any further vetting. Acceptable alternatives for a Canadian bank account is an
OFX, Airwallex, and Wise Global Currency Account, which can offer Merchants local bank
accounts.
3.3.2 Consumer KYC
Consumer KYC
Afterpay Canada has implemented a comprehensive consumer identity verification program to
ensure accurate customer identification and mitigate ML/TF risks.
Consumer Identity Document Verification IDV Program: A risk-based, two-tiered verification
approach:
● Soft ID Check: Primary verification method using data sources such as credit bureau
records
● Hard ID Check: Government-issued photo ID verification for customers unable to complete
soft verification
● Manual Verification: Support process for customers requiring additional assistance
The IDV program incorporates risk assessment to determine appropriate verification levels and
maintains Quebec-specific considerations for biometric data collection. Detailed procedures are
maintained in separate operational documents.
Consumer Risk Rating Integration: Consumer verification integrates with the APCRR framework
to ensure appropriate risk-based measures are applied.

3.4 Customer Risk Rating Models
Afterpay Canada maintains comprehensive customer risk rating frameworks to assess and manage
ML/TF risk across its customer base.
Consumer Risk Rating CRR Framework evaluating consumer attributes including geography,
account characteristics, transaction patterns, and sanctions screening results. Risk ratings are
updated through daily scoring with periodic reviews, supporting risk-based compliance measures.
Merchant Risk Rating MRR Dual methodology combining onboarding risk assessment (aMRR)
with ongoing risk evaluation (cMRR). The framework considers entity structure, geographic
factors, transaction volumes, and compliance history to determine appropriate risk management
measures.
Both frameworks incorporate override mechanisms for sanctions hits, suspicious transaction
reports, and other high-risk indicators, ensuring appropriate escalation and enhanced monitoring
3.5 Discrepancy Management
Discrepancies may occur in relation to Merchant Identification and verification during the
onboarding process. Discrepancies may be categorized as material or immaterial depending on the
classification of the identified data discrepancy and may require additional vetting and escalation
prior to a Merchant being approved for onboarding onto the Afterpay Canada platform.
A discrepancy which is categorized to be material in nature may require the collection of additional
identification information and/or documents to resolve the discrepancy. If a material discrepancy is
unable to be resolved through the collection of additional information from the Merchant, the
Merchant should be declined and the Merchant information forwarded to the North America
Compliance team to determine if further action is required.
Should a discrepancy in Merchant beneficial ownership be categorized as material in nature where
it is assessed to be a high risk of ML/TF, or sanctions evasion offence, the Merchant should be
declined and the information escalated to the Afterpay Canada MLRO without delay. Based on all
the information gathered, the MLRO will make the final determination on whether the discrepancy
is material and of high risk of ML/TF or sanctions evasion. The MLRO will then determine whether
it would be appropriate to report such information to FINTRAC and/or local government agencies.
A discrepancy which is categorized as immaterial is not classified as a discrepancy and therefore
does not require escalation for additional vetting. More detailed information related to
discrepancies related to Merchant identification and verification is set out in the Canada Merchant
Vetting SOP.
Keeping Customer Information Up to Date
Afterpay Canada will review Merchant information and request updated information as needed.
Where Merchant information is determined to require updating, steps may be undertaken to

re-verify the information against reliable electronic databases and/or through collection of
documents. This may include updates and/or re-verification of information relating to the Merchant
beneficial ownership information or Merchant bank details.
High Risk Merchants may need regular reviews in accordance with the Merchant Enhanced Due
Diligence Procedure to ensure the information remains accurate and up to date.
Audit and Compliance
Afterpay Canada reserves the right to conduct periodic audits or compliance reviews of its
partners to assess their adherence to AML/CTF requirements. This ensures ongoing compliance
and identifies any areas that need improvement.
3.6 Transaction Monitoring & Reporting
Although Afterpay Canada is not a reporting entity under the PCMLTFA, and is therefore not legally
obligated to submit STRs or report to FINTRAC, it has adopted certain voluntary measures aligned
with industry best practices to detect and respond to suspicious activity.
3.6.1 Merchant Monitoring
Any monitoring of Merchant transaction activity will be for the purpose of identifying potentially
suspicious activity, which may include:
● Complex, unusual, or large transactions, including;
○ Transactions of an unusually large size or volume relative to the Merchant profile
(or usual Merchant behaviour);
○ Transactions that exceed internal thresholds and triggers;
○ Irregular patterns of account activity that are characteristic of fraud, ML/TF; and
● Unusual patterns of transactions
● Risk rating assessments to identify activities requiring further investigation
3.6.2 Consumer Monitoring
Similarly, any monitoring of Consumer transaction activity will be for the purpose of identifying
potentially suspicious activity, which may also include:
● Complex, unusual, or large transactions, including;
○ Transactions of an unusually large size or volume relative to the Consumer profile
(or usual Consumer behaviour);
○ Transactions that exceed internal thresholds and triggers;
○ Irregular patterns of account activity that are characteristic of fraud, ML/TF; and
● Unusual patterns of transactions
● Risk rating assessments to identify activities requiring further investigation

If a Merchant or Consumer transaction activity is flagged as potentially suspicious, or there are
reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction (attempted or completed) is related to the
commission or the attempted commission of fraud, ML/TF, or sanctions evasion, such transaction
activity will be escalated to the MLRO or designee for further investigation and review.
Based on all the information gathered, the MLRO will make the final determination on whether the
transaction activity is suspicious and whether it would be appropriate to report such transaction
activity to FINTRAC and/or local law enforcement authorities.
3.7 Sanctions Screening & Compliance with Sanctions Laws
As a Canadian incorporated entity, Afterpay Canada has legal obligations under Canadian
sanctions legislation and must comply with all applicable sanctions measures. Afterpay Canada will
screen all Merchants and Consumers against the Canadian Designated Lists under the Canadian
sanctions laws and will take a risk-based approach to collecting and screening the names of the
beneficial owners of Merchants in its network.
Screening Requirements: All customers are screened against Canadian Designated Lists under
applicable sanctions legislation. Enhanced screening procedures incorporate:
● Russia and Iran Ministerial Directive requirements treating relevant transactions as high-risk
● Counter-proliferation sanctions guidance for dual-use goods and complex corporate
structures
● Enhanced due diligence for high-risk jurisdictions and entities
Reporting Obligations: Sanctions hits and suspected sanctions evasion must be reported to
relevant Canadian authorities such as the Royal Canadian Mounted Police RCMP, the Canadian
Security Intelligence Service CSIS, and FINTRAC as required by law.
Enhanced Measures: Additional scrutiny and comprehensive documentation applies to
transactions involving high-risk jurisdictions, complex ownership structures, and activities that may
indicate sanctions evasion attempts.
3.7.1 Diligence and Sanctions Screening
Afterpay Canada will screen all Merchant and Consumer names against appropriate watch lists,
which at a minimum include the lists of persons designated under Canadian Sanctions Legislation
(the “Canadian Designated Listsˮ). All screening will take place at the initiation of the relationship
with a Merchant or Consumer, and periodically thereafter as determined by the MLRO.
Afterpay Canada may also rely on a commercial sanctions database to carry out this screening.
The MLRO is responsible for ensuring that any commercial sanctions database or third party
vendor used is suitable and for verifying that any sanctions database relied on by such vendor (a)
contains all of the Canadian Designated Lists and (b) is updated in a timely manner to include
newly designated persons or applicable statutory or regulatory changes. This verification will be
done periodically, as required, and at least annually. Afterpay Canada may screen others, as

warranted based on risk and, as determined by the MLRO. Such persons may include guarantors
(to the extent applicable), beneficial owners of Merchants, suppliers and vendors.
All potential matches are reported immediately to the MLRO or designee for further evaluation. If
the MLRO or designee determines a hit to be a false match, the review will be memorialized and
maintained in accordance with applicable record keeping procedures. In circumstances where it is
determined that there is a likely match against the Canadian Designated List, the match will be
referred to the MLRO (if reviewed by a designee) who will ensure that the relationship with the
Merchant, Consumer or third-party is frozen and determine the required next steps, if necessary.
Where a determination is made that a positive hit or “True Matchˮ has occurred against the
Canadian Designated List, the MLRO will promptly report this to the relevant regulatory authority. In
instances where sanctions evasion is suspected, on the part of either a Merchant or a Consumer,
these cases will also be escalated to the MLRO, who will promptly report to FINTRAC and the
required local law enforcement authorities.
3.7.2 Listed Person or Entity Property Reporting
If Afterpay Canada believes that a transaction is related to property owned or controlled by or on
behalf of a terrorist or a terrorist group, the MLRO should promptly investigate. This involves
collaboration with authorities and analysis of transaction patterns and Customer profiles. If
suspicion is confirmed, the MLRO must prevent the transaction, freeze related property and file a
Listed Person or Entity Property Report in addition to a STR with FINTRAC, providing detailed
information for investigation.
3.7.3 Disclosure to the RCMP and/or CSIS
The MLRO will be responsible for reporting without delay to the Royal Canadian Mounted Police
("RCMP") and/or Canadian Security Intelligence Service ("CSIS") the existence of property in
Afterpay Canada's possession or control that Afterpay Canada has reason to believe is owned or
controlled, directly or indirectly, by a person on the Canadian Designated Lists or by any other
person with whom dealings are prohibited under Canadian Sanctions Legislation, together with
information about the underlying transaction or attempted transaction.

3.8 Tipping Off
Tipping off to a Consumer/Merchant is not applicable to Afterpay Canada as it is not a reporting
entity to FINTRAC. Nonetheless, Afterpay Canada generally prohibits Employees from disclosing

know basis.
3.9 Information Sharing and Reporting
Afterpay Canadaʼs AML Program aligns with the regulatory framework established by the FINTRAC.
In accordance with the PCMLTFA, Afterpay Canada and its partners are bound by contractual
obligations to facilitate effective information sharing and reporting. This Program emphasizes
compliance with FINTRACʼs guidelines, including the reporting of suspicious transactions and the
monitoring of Customer activities.
Partners are required to adhere to the reporting obligations outlined in Part 1 of the PCMLTFA,
promptly submitting STRs when necessary. This collaborative approach ensures that Afterpay
Canada and its partners contribute to the broader national efforts to combat money laundering and
uphold the integrity of the Canadian financial system. Afterpay Canadaʼs commitment to regulatory
compliance not only strengthens the AML Program but also demonstrates Afterpay Canadaʼs
dedication to operating within the legal framework established by Canadian authorities.
3.10 Compliance with Law Enforcement
If Afterpay Canada receives either a formal or informal request to produce information from a
Canadian law enforcement officer or agency, it is Afterpay Canadaʼs policy to cooperate and
comply with such requests for information or assistance, to the extent permitted by applicable law.
The MLRO is responsible for coordinating Afterpay Canadaʼs compliance with such requests.
Afterpay Canadaʼs policy to cooperate with requests from Canadian law enforcement aligns with
legal obligations, aiding investigations related to financial crimes such as ML/TF, fostering
regulatory compliance, and demonstrating corporate responsibility in supporting law enforcement
efforts.

3.11 Record Keeping
The PCMLTFA mandates that reporting entities, including financial institutions and entities engaged
in certain businesses, maintain records related to their AML/CTF programs for a specified period of
time.
Afterpay Canada will maintain relevant data, information and documents relating to the AML
Program for a minimum period of seven 7 years. More specifically, this related to the following:
● Records related to transactional data should be retained for a period of seven years after
the date of the transaction;
● Records of the customer identification procedures used to identify a Merchant for the
duration of the relationship and an additional seven years after the termination of the
relationship;
● Records of consumer identity document verification IDV procedures including soft ID
checks, hard ID checks, and manual verification processes for the duration of the
relationship and an additional seven years after account closure;
● Records of beneficial ownership identification and verification for merchants, including
ownership structure documentation and periodic updates, for the duration of the
relationship and an additional seven years after the termination of the relationship;
● Records of customer risk rating assessments CRR/MRR and updates for the duration of
the relationship and an additional seven years after the termination of the relationship;
● Records of discrepancy management and resolution for merchant identification and
verification for a period of seven years after resolution;
● Records of Corporations Canada database consultations and material discrepancy
reporting for beneficial ownership verification for a period of seven years after the
consultation;
● Records of sanctions screening results, match evaluations, and actions taken on blocked or
frozen assets will be kept for five 5 years after the date the property was unblocked; and
● Records relating to the adoption of the AML Program (including any ML/TF risk
assessments) and any amendments made to the Program for a period of seven years after
it has ceased to apply.
Afterpay Canada will also retain records that assist in demonstrating its compliance with the
Blockʼs Global Policies and Program for a minimum of seven years. This includes, but is not limited
to, records relating to Employee Due Diligence and AML/CTF risk awareness training programs.

4.5. Exceptions
The Program Owner or designee will have authority to approve exceptions and/or exemptions to
this Policy, provided that the proposed exception or exemption does not violate applicable laws
and regulations. Exception requests must be documented in writing, include a reason for a request,
and provide a description of the controls that the Company will implement to mitigate risks
associated with the exception. Any approved exceptions must be documented in writing and
retained with recordkeeping requirements. Any approved exceptions or exemptions should be
submitted to the Policy Governance Team. The Document Owner and/or designee will inform
Compliance Leadership and applicable committees, as needed, of exceptions.

may elect to escalate the request for an exception to the appropriate risk and compliance
governance forum. The MLRO or designee will keep a record of all exceptions requested and the
outcome for this Program.
Staff members may submit exception requests for review at go/policyhelp.

Please note that the following is provided for informational purposes only and is not meant to serve
as legal advice. Escalate any situation-specific inquiries to the MLRO or their designee.
1. What is money laundering?
Money laundering is generally defined as engaging in acts designed to conceal or disguise the true
origins of money, the proceeds of which were derived from carrying out illegal activities, so that
such proceeds appear to have derived from legitimate origins or constitute legitimate assets. There
are normally three stages in money laundering.
● “Placementˮ Stage – where the illegally earned money is introduced to the financial system
(e.g., by depositing into a bank account, converted into monetary instruments such as
money orders or travellerʼs checks).
● “Layeringˮ Stage – where the money is converted into a different type of asset (e.g.,
converting a bank deposit to an unsecured note) or moved to different financial institutions,
in each case, for the purposes of further separating the money from its criminal origin.
● “Integrationˮ Stage – where the money is “legitimatelyˮ integrated back into the mainstream
economy (e.g., by purchasing real estate).
2. What is terrorism financing?
Terrorist financing may not involve the proceeds of criminal conduct, but instead may entail an
attempt to conceal either the origin of the funds or their intended use, which could be for criminal
purposes. Terrorism financing often involves money raised from legitimate sources, such as
donations and business profits as well as money from illegal sources to fund terrorism. Those
involved in terrorism financing often use techniques similar to money launderers, but the funding
does not always require large sums of money and the associated transactions may not be
complex.
3. What is sanctions evasion?
Canadian sanctions place restrictions on the activities permissible between persons in Canada or
Canadians outside Canada and foreign states, individuals, or entities. Sanctions evasion is an
offence arising from the contravention of a restriction or prohibition established by an order or a
regulation made under the United Nations Act, the Special Economic Measures Act or the
Justice for Victims of Corrupt Foreign Officials Act Sergei Magnitsky Law). Sanctions are
subject to change without notice and listings are updated as needed to ensure it includes the
most accurate and up to date information.
