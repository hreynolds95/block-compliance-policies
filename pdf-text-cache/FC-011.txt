1. Overview
Introduction and Purpose: Squareup Europe Limited (“SELˮ) is an authorised Electronic Money
Institution, regulated and supervised by the Financial Conduct Authority (“FCAˮ) in the United
Kingdom UK, under Firm Reference Number 900846. The direct parent company is Squareup
International Limited, registered in the Republic of Ireland; both companies are wholly owned

SELʼs two key product offerings are (i) Square Managed Payments and (ii) Cash App (in Winddown

Block, Inc.
Block, Inc., a Delaware corporation, is a merchant services aggregator and mobile payments
company based in San Francisco, California in the United States of America. It is registered as a
Money Services Business with the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (“FinCENˮ).
SELʼs primary Cash App product offering is a peer-to-peer money transfer service, allowing
individuals, through a mobile software application, to transfer funds via stored balance or from a
debit card issued by a UK-based financial institution to another individualʼs Cash App account. A
feature of Cash App is the ability to make cross-currency transactions (send and receive funds
from other jurisdictions where Cash App is available). This feature enables individual Cash App
users to transfer funds between Cash App accounts e.g., a US individual Cash App customer can
transfer US dollars from their stored value balance or linked debit card to a UK individual
customerʼs Cash App account where it will appear in her GBP stored value balance, and vice versa.
Scope: This Financial Crime Program document applies to Cash App products available in the UK.

Definitions:
Terminology Definition
Money Laundering Money Laundering is the process by which individuals or organisations
conceal the origins of illegally obtained funds, making them appear
legitimate. In the context of SEL, Money Laundering typically involves
disguising the proceeds of crime through legitimate payment
channels, including merchant transactions and card processing
services. Criminals may attempt to exploit financial institutions to
move, layer or integrate illicit funds into the financial system. As a
regulated entity under the UK Money Laundering Regulations, SEL has
a legal and ethical responsibility to detect and prevent such activities
by implementing robust Anti-Money Laundering (“AMLˮ) controls,
including customer due diligence (“CDDˮ), transaction monitoring and
the reporting of suspicious activity.
Terrorist Financing Terrorist Financing involves the collection or provision of funds with
the intention or knowledge that they will be used to support terrorist
acts, individuals or organisations. Unlike Money Laundering, the origin
of the funds used for terrorist financing may be legitimate or illicit,
making detection more complex. For SEL, there is a heightened risk
that payment processing services could be misused to raise, move or
store funds that support terrorism, often through seemingly legitimate
merchants or transactions. Under the Terrorism Act 2000 (“TACTˮ)
and related UK regulations, SEL must implement effective controls to
identify and report suspicious activity, conduct enhanced due
diligence (“EDDˮ) where necessary and ensure systems are in place to
prevent its services from being exploited for terrorist financing
purposes.
Proliferation Financing Proliferation Financing refers to the act of providing funds or financial
services that are used to support the development, acquisition or
transfer of weapons of mass destruction (“WMDsˮ), including nuclear,
chemical or biological weapons. This type of financing is often linked
to sanctioned individuals, entities or countries and may involve
complex networks designed to evade international controls. For SEL,
the risk lies in unintentionally facilitating transactions that contribute to
proliferation activities through merchant relationships or payment
processing. Under UK regulations and international obligations such as
UN and UK sanctions regimes, SEL is required to implement robust
screening, due diligence and monitoring systems to detect and
prevent any involvement in Proliferation Financing, ensuring
compliance with all applicable sanctions and export control laws.

2. Legal & Regulatory Framework
The UK Money Laundering Regulations, applying to financial institutions, date from 1993, the
current Regulations being the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds
Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (as amended) Money Laundering Regulations 2017.
The offence of Money Laundering was contained in various acts of parliament (such as the
Criminal Justice Act 1988 and the Drug Trafficking Offences Act 1986. The Proceeds of Crime Act
2002 (“POCAˮ) consolidated, updated and reformed the law relating to money laundering to
include any dealing in criminal property. Specific obligations to combat terrorist financing were set
out in the Terrorism Act 2000.
SEL takes into account legislation such as the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, the Money Laundering,
Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (as
amended) , the Counter Terrorism Act 2008, the Electronic Money Regulations 2011 and the
Payment Services Regulations 2017. As an e-money institution, SEL must also comply with the
relevant parts of the FCA handbook for firms and take into account the FCAʼs Approach Document.

SELʼs business is subject to a wide range of laws and regulations within the UK and the US,
including Anti-Money Laundering and Counter Terrorist Financing and and Counter Proliferation
Financing AML/CTF/CPF legislation as well as US Treasury Departmentʼs Office of Foreign Assets
Control (“OFACˮ) and US sanctions requirements. SEL also follows the international framework
proposed by the Financial Conduct Authority (“FCAˮ), Financial Action Task Force (“FATFˮ) and
guidance produced by Joint Money Laundering Steering Group (“JMLSGˮ) in the UK.
3. Financial Crime Program Summary
This document outlines SELʼs comprehensive Financial Crime Program, which is designed to
identify, assess and mitigate risks related to Financial Crime. It encompasses the key components
of SELʼs framework to prevent and detect Money Laundering (“MLˮ), Terrorist Financing (“TFˮ) and
Proliferation Financing (“PFˮ).
SEL is committed to developing, implementing, and maintaining an effective, risk based Financial
Crime Program, which is reasonably designed to prevent Cash App from being used to facilitate
ML, TF or PF activities.
This Financial Crime Program document applies to all the Cash App products available in the UK.
The objectives of this Financial Crime program for the UK are:
● To develop and implement a risk-based Financial Crime program for all Cash App activities;
● To develop risk-based AML/CTF/CPF control procedures for all Cash App activities;
● To promote awareness of applicable AML/CTF/CPF laws and regulations;
● To detect, prevent, monitor, and report any suspicious activity and TFtransactions; and
● Fully cooperate with all law enforcement, regulatory agencies, and financial intelligence
units to the extent required and permitted under applicable law.
It is vital that all SEL personnel1 are aware of and committed to achieving compliance with all
applicable requirements of the Financial Crime program, laws, and regulations. All SEL employees
and support staff, including employees of other Block entities who support Cash App in the UK,
are expected to follow the protocols and processes outlined in this Program document, as well as
any related procedures, to ensure compliance with all related legal and regulatory requirements.

5. Risk assessment and Management
Regulation 18 and 18A of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 requires firms to Identify and
assess the ML/TF/PF risks to which its firm is subject to and should apply a risk-based approach
when applying AML/CTF/CPF compliance measures. SEL assesses ML/TF/PF risk which it is
exposed to resulting from the nature and complexity of the business.
In order to develop appropriate systems and controls, SEL assesses AML/CTF/CPF risks, both
specific and general, that arise in relation to the customers, products and services, delivery
channels, geography and other risks as identified by the national risk assessment, and risks
identified by SEL but not contained in the above categories. SEL also recognises other significant
risks identified by the JMLSG, FATF, FCA and other relevant government agencies such as
sanctions, bribery, corruption, and fraud.

SEL implements a risk-based approach to focus effort where it is needed and where it will be most
effective in countering the risks posed to SEL and the ecosystem in which it operates. Risk
assessment and management is an ongoing process conducted locally and at a global level. It is
continuously evaluated so that the greatest resources are assigned appropriately to those areas
that pose the greatest risk.
The Risk Assessment Program incorporates identification of gaps and weaknesses, developing
action plans, and tracking those action plans to completion. Cash Appʼs risk assessment team in
collaboration with the MLRO, will track enhancements and formalise the changes required into an
action plan. As new risks are identified or presented, consideration will be given to the
modification of the program and strategies to execute upon the program.
Before new products or services are launched, the business must review the product or service for
potential ML/TF/PF risks and ensure appropriate controls are implemented if such risks exist.
The UK MLRO
● Conducts and documents a risk assessment and analysis for ML, TF and PF risks related to
the products and services offered. A risk assessment and analysis is required no less than
annually and may be conducted more frequently, if required.
● Presents results of the risk assessment to the Board of Directors annually.
● Incorporates recommendations from the results of the risk assessment into this Financial
Crime Program and, where applicable, controls into the relevant products or services.
6. Know your Customer
Regulation 27 & 28 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 require firms to determine the level
of CDD to be applied on a risk based approach. SEL uses a formal process to obtain information
on its customers to reasonably assess their identity and legitimacy, as required by law and in line
with SELʼs risk based approach.
The nature of Cash Appʼs businesses is that customers send and receive P2P payments. The due
diligence checks and processes are built into the onboarding process that a customer completes
to sign up and use the Cash App product and its services. The verification of a customerʼs identity
is conducted at the start of the business relationship through a third party tool for identification and
verification (“IDVˮ) purposes.
A risk profile is assigned to the customer at onboarding to allow for greater attention to certain risk
levels to ensure appropriate due diligence is carried out.
If adverse information is found on an individual during the onboarding process, the Compliance
Team reviews all information available and makes a determination as to whether such information
precludes an approval, based on Cash Appʼs Terms of Service and its Financial Crime Program.
Following the review, a decision to approve or decline the customer is made, along with the
documented rationale.SEL maintains KYC processes and procedures in line with this Financial
Crime Program.

7. Customer Risk Rating Model
The cornerstone of a strong AML compliance program is the adoption and implementation of
risk-based due diligence policies and procedures for all customers, particularly those that present
a higher risk for ML,TF and PF. In order to achieve this, SEL has designed and implemented a CRR
program that assesses a customer's profile and assigns risk rating and monitors the risk levels of
the customers.
The CRR program enables SEL to apply the appropriate risk-based controls to mitigate exposure to
money launderers, terrorist financing, and financial crime. Improper assessment of a customerʼs
risk can have a cascading effect, creating deficiencies in multiple areas of internal controls and
resulting in an overall weakened AML compliance program.
8. Politically Exposed Persons
A Politically Exposed Person (‘PEPʼ) is defined in Regulation 3512a & 14 of the Money
Laundering Regulations 2017 as an individual who is or has been entrusted with prominent public
functions, other than as a middle-ranking or more junior official. This includes both domestic and
non-domestic PEPs. It also includes their family and known close associates.
Family includes:
● Spouse or partner of the person;
● Children of the person, their spouses or partners; and
● Parents of that person.
A known close associate is an individual:
● Known to have joint beneficial ownership of a legal entity or legal arrangement or any other
close business relations with a PEP; or
● Who has sole beneficial ownership of a legal entity or a legal arrangement which is known
to have been set up for the benefit of a PEP.
SEL is not precluded from establishing business relationships with PEPs, but it recognises that
PEPs potentially pose a higher ML and TF risk because individuals holding such positions may
misuse their power or influence for personal gain or advantage. PEPs may also use family or close
associates to hide funds or assets obtained through the abuse of their position or bribery and
corruption. SEL is aligned with the FCA guidance of the definition of a PEP and who should be
treated as a PEP

12. Enhanced Due Diligence
SEL has designed an EDD monitoring process as part of its KYC Program and inline with the
requirements detailed in Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. Customers
identified that could potentially pose an ML/TF/PF or other financial crime risk undergo EDDe,
meaning the accounts are reviewed with additional scrutiny and frequency once the risk is
identified. Customers are identified primarily through the CRR model.
An EDD review will be triggered if an account meets one or more of the below criteria or direction
is received from the MLRO/DMLRO
● Accounts with confirmed Law enforcement hits;
● Accounts with confirmed negative news hits;
● Accounts with confirmed PEP/RCA Relative or Close Associate) hits;
● Customers identified through the CRR; or
● A Customer or transactions present a higher risk of ML/TF/PF.
The MLRO/DMLRO reserves the right to mandate EDD at any point during the customer
relationship, regardless of whether a trigger event has occurred. This discretionary authority
allows the MLRO/DMLRO to respond dynamically to emerging risks, intelligence or regulatory
requirements.
The analyst reviews all information available, as part of the EDD review and on completing their
review, escalates all EDD cases where enforcement hits or PEP hits are confirmed, and all EDD
cases where suspicious activity has been detected, to the MLRO/DMLRO for approval. The MLROʼs
decision to approve or decline the customer is then made, along with the documented rationale.

13. Monitoring Program
SEL monitors the activity of customers to identify any transactions, patterns, or behaviours which
appear to be potentially suspicious and related to ML/TF/PF. SEL uses a combination of analytic
models, in-house front-end software applications, vendor supported analytics,3 and manual
analysis to review activity on an ongoing basis from the beginning to the end of the business
relationship.
The analytic models apply a set of curated algorithms to run against the transaction data set and
populate a set of review queues. The Risk Management Team reviews possible fraud related
activity via their independent queues, with escalation of ML to the Compliance Team. The analytic
models employ a dynamic, risk-based approach taking into account a number of factors unique to
the customer base. The Compliance Team reviews the algorithms using ongoing analysis of
transaction data, information from the risk assessment, and takes into account the type of product
and service in use.
The risks factor include, but are not limited to:
● Any transaction that appears to have no apparent economic or visible lawful purpose;
● Any unusual patterns of transactions that have no apparent economic or visible lawful
purpose;
● Any unusual patterns of transaction involving linked customers, assets, or information;
● Payment instrument diversity in the transaction mix for the customer;
● Multiple payments from the same card or large payments;
● Personal identification provided by the customer is the same as that submitted by other
persons opening an account or other customers;
● Discrepancies in the provided personal identifying information when compared against
external information sources used by the SEL; and
● Personal identifying information provided is of a type commonly associated with fraudulent
activity as indicated by internal or third-party sources used by SEL;
The output from the analytic models are reviewed using a set of factors to determine whether the
activity is potentially suspicious.The baseline factors are a set of common red flag indicators
based on AML laws and best practices.
The factors include, but are not limited to:
● Customers who exhibit an unusual amount of concern regarding SELʼs KYC procedures and
reporting requirements, particularly if they demonstrate reluctance to reveal their identity
or provide relevant documentation about their business or transactions;
● Customers who engage in transactions inconsistent with their anticipated activity or stated
business type (for example: unsubstantiated velocity of transactions, irregular transaction
amounts, times, and locations);
● individuals who engage in inconsistent transaction patterns or activity (for example:
unsubstantiated velocity of transactions, irregular transaction amounts, times, and
locations);

14. Reporting Suspicious Activity
The purpose of this section is to define the responsibilities and legal obligations for identifying and
reporting of suspicious activity in compliance with;
● Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 POCA;
● Terrorism Act 2000;

● Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Information on the Payer)
Regulations 2017;
● FCA Handbook SYSC 6.3.7; and
● National Crime Agency (“NCAˮ) SARs Regime guidance
s330 POCA requires SEL to report potentially suspicious transactions or activity by completing a
suspicious activity or transaction report, within the relevant timeframe, and to collect and maintain
supporting documentation in accordance with the record retention requirements.
SEL files a suspicious activity report and notifies law enforcement of all customers, transactions,
and activity that raise an identifiable suspicion of criminal, terrorist, fraudulent, or corrupt activity
via the SAR filing process, including tax evasion.
Where such suspicions arise, Staff must promptly submit an Internal SAR to the MLRO/DMLRO as
soon as practicable. This is known as internal escalation and the report must be completed using
the SEL designated internal escalation SAR process.
The MLRO/DMLRO upon receipt of such an internal report will evaluate whether the suspicion
meets the legal threshold for filing a SAR with the UK Financial Intelligence Unit (“UKFIUˮ), which
forms part of the NCA.
Failure to comply with these reporting obligations may constitute a criminal offence, including
under Section 330 of POCA, which pertains to the failure to disclose.
Once this reporting has been completed by the employee, contractor, director, officer, or agent,
they have discharged their personal obligation in this respect and the MLRO/DMLRO is to
acknowledge receipt of such reporting. SEL maintains SAR processes and procedures in line with
this Financial Crime Program.
The MLRO/DMLRO is responsible for maintaining a secure and auditable log of all internal reports,
external SARs Submissions to the NCA and related documentation. This includes any
communications with NCA, Law enforcement agencies or regulators. Records must be retained for
a minimum period of five years following the end of the business relationship or the date of the
report, whichever is later. These records must be readily available for inspection by the FCA or
other competent authorities upon request.
14.1 Internal Escalations
Internal escalation of suspicious activity or transactions can be reported to the MLRO through

which is monitored by the Compliance and Risk Management Teams. If the reporter does not have

14.2 Regulatory Reporting

14.3 Confidentially & Tipping Off
According to section 333A POCA Section, and section 21D of the Terrorism Act 2000 and
Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 Amendment) Regulations 2007, SEL can commit a tipping-off offence
if it does anything that may tip off another person that a suspicion has been raised, discloses to a
third person that a SAR has been filed, or discloses that an investigation into an offence is being
contemplated or carried out if SELʼs disclosure is likely to prejudice any criminal investigation.
Accordingly, all SARs must be kept confidential. Confidential information also includes any
documentation stating that a SAR was or was not filed and the basis on which the decision was
made.
All SAR contemplation, resulting in filing to the NCA or a no-SAR decision SAR recorded internally
only), must not be disclosed or even hinted to the Customer as well as all other subjects of the
SAR, or anyone within SEL, SELʼs parent company, or any of its affiliated companies that does not
have a need to know of such actions.
SEL holds SARs and any supporting documentation in a confidential folder. SEL does not inform
anyone outside of a law enforcement or regulatory agency about the details of a SAR. In addition,
the details of a SAR filings and copies of supporting documentation are segregated from other SEL
records to avoid disclosing SAR details.
14.4 Law Enforcement & Regulatory Bodies
SEL responds to any law enforcement, regulatory bodies, government agency, and other financial
institution requests for documentation on customers by searching its records to determine
whether it has engaged in any transaction with the individual or entity named in the request. If a
match is found, it is reported to the appropriate authority. If the search does not uncover a match,
then SEL will reply in accordance with local laws and regulations.
SEL does not disclose the fact that there has been a request to any customers, except to the extent
necessary to comply with the information request from a law enforcement or regulatory body.
SELʼs Compliance function does not use information provided for any purpose other than 1 to
report to appropriate law enforcement or government agency; 2 to determine whether to
establish or maintain a relationship; or 3 to assist SEL in complying with any information sharing
regulatory requirements.
The MLRO regularly reviews the content of such reports to:
● Determine whether SARS are warranted to be submitted to the NCA;
● Consider whether the monitoring program for SEL needs refinement, in the light of the
intelligence from these requests; and
● Report to the Board of Directors and senior management on the nature of such activity
occurring utilising SELʼs infrastructure.

AML functions outsourced intra-group, including KYC, IDV, CDD, Transaction Monitoring, Sanctions
Screening, Suspicious Activity Investigation and Customer Complaints, are overseen by the UK
Head of Compliance/MLRO.
18. Regular Independent Assessment
SELʼs Financial Crime Program is assessed for effectiveness through regulatory examinations,
audits, and independent reviews, as required. The MLRO will assign the audit responsibility to a
person who is both independent and has the experience and skill to conduct a thorough and
effective independent review. The reviewer can be a member of SEL or Block, Inc. Staff with audit
or internal control experience, an outside auditor, or some other independent professional.
Currently, the MLRO has assigned the responsibility to SELʼs Internal Audit team who in turn have
contracted with one of the Big 4 External Auditing firms4 to conduct the annual assessment.
The MLRO, senior management, and the Board of Directors will receive a written summary of the
results of the audit, recommending corrective action, and monitoring compliance with those
recommendations. The Board of Directors shall hold the Head of Compliance/MLRO and senior
management accountable for the existence of any deficiencies and prompt resolution of such
deficiencies identified in the audit report.
19. Compliance
19.1 Roles and Responsibilities
● Money Laundering Reporting Officer: SELʼs Board has appointed a MLRO, who leads the
compliance effort and has overall responsibility for the execution of the Financial Crime
Program in the UK. The MLRO has the full authorisation and approval from the Board of
Directors and senior management of SEL to act independently of them to fulfil the role. The
MLRO has sufficient and appropriate AML/CTF/CPF knowledge and expertise. The MLRO
has autonomy and has the ability to escalate matters to the Board if required.
The MLRO is principally responsible for:

19.2 Training
New Hire Training
At a company-wide level, all new employees receive AML/CTF/CPF training, through an online
based interactive training course. The online based interactive training has a comprehensive
assessment at the end of the course. The AML/CTF/CPF training course is updated at a minimum
annually.
Refresher Training
All employees are required to take refresher training on an annual basis. The AML/CTF/CPF
training is designed to enable the employees to understand:
● What activities amount to ML,TF and PF and how they should be reported;
● Key elements of the AML/CTF/CPF laws including coverage of;

○ developing and maintaining a Financial Crime Program and the key features of
such;
○ SELʼs KYC obligations;
○ SELʼs reporting obligations; and
○ record keeping and retention obligations.
● The type of ML/TF/PF risks that we might face and how employees are able to spot these
risks; and
● The consequences of non-compliance with the AML/CTF/CPF laws, on SEL and its
employees; and
● The effect on society of the failure to implement good Financial Crime Programs.
Targeted AML/CTF/CPF
SEL provides more in-depth AML training programs to:
● Local or non local Customer Success teams;
● Business, operations, marketing and product teams;
● Board of Directors, in person/via video conference (on annual basis);
● Compliance personnel whereby a training program is tailored to their compliance role and
regulatory issues and risk specific to their region. Training may include:
○ Webinars provided by established AML organisations such as Association of
Certified Anti-Money Laundering Specialists ACAMS or the International
Compliance Association ICA;
○ government entities;
○ Distribution of written materials;
○ AML conferences;
○ Department meetings; or
○ Other internally developed training sessions.
Additionally, processes are thoroughly documented enabling new employees to quickly and
effectively learn and perform their core job functions.
19.3 Record Retention
All Documents are retained according to the longer of:
● Block's Data Classification & Handling Policy
● Applicable regulatory requirements
Additionally, SELʼs policy is to maintain for at least five years from the end of the business
relationship all necessary records, as it relates to Customers, but not limited to the following:
● Electronic record of, a copy or extract of the records as it relates to the KYC program;
● Supporting documentation as it relates to the customer transaction activity; and
● Monitoring and reporting of potentially suspicious activity.
SEL considers the end of the business relationship to occur when the final transaction is
processed or when the customer terminates its business relationship with SEL.

In addition SEL will maintain for at least five years, the following information as it relates to the SEL
business:
● Employee training records;
● Financial Crime Program documentation and supporting material; and
● Reports under the supervision of the MLRO (for example, reporting to the senior
management, Board of Directors, and FCA.
When applicable, SEL maintains records in compliance with regulations where retention
requirements are longer than SEL's own policies. The MLRO will be responsible for ensuring that
the appropriate records are maintained.
19.4 Exceptions
The Program Owner or designee will have authority to approve exceptions and/or exemptions to
this Policy, provided that the proposed exception or exemption does not violate applicable laws
and regulations. Exception requests must be documented in writing, include a reason for a request,
and provide a description of the controls that the Company will implement to mitigate risks
associated with the exception. Any approved exceptions must be documented in writing and
retained with recordkeeping requirements. Any approved exceptions or exemptions should be
submitted to the Policy Governance Team. The Document Owner and/or designee will inform
Compliance Leadership and applicable committees, as needed, of exceptions.
