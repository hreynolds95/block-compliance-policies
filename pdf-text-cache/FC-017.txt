Definitions:
Terminology Definition
Money Laundering Money Laundering is the process by which individuals or organisations
conceal the origins of illegally obtained funds, making them appear

legitimate. In the context of SEL, Money Laundering typically involves
disguising the proceeds of crime through legitimate payment channels,
including merchant transactions and card processing services. Criminals
may attempt to exploit financial institutions to move, layer or integrate illicit
funds into the financial system. As a regulated entity under the UK Money
Laundering Regulations, SEL has a legal and ethical responsibility to detect
and prevent such activities by implementing robust Anti-Money Laundering
(“AMLˮ) controls, including customer due diligence (“CDDˮ), transaction
monitoring and the reporting of suspicious activity.
Terrorist Financing Terrorist Financing involves the collection or provision of funds with the
intention or knowledge that they will be used to support terrorist acts,
individuals or organisations. Unlike Money Laundering, the origin of the
funds used for terrorist financing may be legitimate or illicit, making
detection more complex. For SEL, there is a heightened risk that payment
processing services could be misused to raise, move or store funds that
support terrorism, often through seemingly legitimate merchants or
transactions. Under the Terrorism Act 2000 (“TACTˮ) and related UK
regulations, SEL must implement effective controls to identify and report
suspicious activity, conduct enhanced due diligence (“EDDˮ) where
necessary and ensure systems are in place to prevent its services from
being exploited for terrorist financing purposes.
Proliferation Proliferation Financing refers to the act of providing funds or financial
Financing services that are used to support the development, acquisition or transfer
of weapons of mass destruction (“WMDsˮ), including nuclear, chemical or
biological weapons. This type of financing is often linked to sanctioned
individuals, entities or countries and may involve complex networks
designed to evade international controls. For SEL, the risk lies in
unintentionally facilitating transactions that contribute to proliferation
activities through merchant relationships or payment processing. Under UK
regulations and international obligations such as UN and UK sanctions
regimes, SEL is required to implement robust screening, due diligence and
monitoring systems to detect and prevent any involvement in Proliferation
Financing, ensuring compliance with all applicable sanctions and export
control laws.

2. Legal & Regulatory Framework
The UK Money Laundering Regulations, applying to financial institutions, date from 1993, the
current Regulations being the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds
Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (as amended) Money Laundering Regulations 2017.
The offence of money laundering was contained in various acts of parliament (such as the Criminal
Justice Act 1988 and the Drug Trafficking Offences Act 1986. The Proceeds of Crime Act 2002
(“POCAˮ) consolidated, updated and reformed the law relating to money laundering to include any
dealing in criminal property. Specific obligations to combat terrorist financing were set out in the
Terrorism Act 2000.
SEL takes into account legislation such as the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, the Money Laundering,
Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (as
amended) , the Counter Terrorism Act 2008, the Electronic Money Regulations 2011 and the
Payment Services Regulations 2017. As an e-money institution, SEL must also comply with the
relevant parts of the FCA handbook for firms and take into account the FCAʼs Approach Document.

5. Risk assessment and Management
Regulation 18 and 18A of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 requires firms to Identify and
assess the risks Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Proliferation Financing to which its
firm is subject to and should apply a risk-based approach when applying AML/CTF/CPF
compliance measures. SEL assesses ML/TF/PF risk which it is exposed to resulting from the nature
and complexity of the business.
In order to develop appropriate systems and controls, SEL assesses AML/CTF/CPF risks, both
specific and general, that arise in relation to the customers, products and services, delivery
channels, geography and other risks as identified by the national risk assessment, and risks
identified by SEL but not contained in the above categories. SEL also recognises other significant
risks identified by the JMLSG, FATF, FCA and other relevant government agencies such as
sanctions, bribery, corruption, and fraud.
SEL implements a risk-based approach to focus effort where it is needed and where it will be most
effective in countering the risks posed to SEL and the ecosystem in which it operates. Risk
assessment and management is an ongoing process conducted locally and at a global level. It is
continuously evaluated so that the greatest resources are assigned appropriately to those areas
that pose the greatest risk.

The Risk Assessment Program incorporates the identification of gaps and weaknesses, developing
action plans, and tracking those action plans to completion. Squareʼs risk assessment team in
collaboration with the MLRO, will track enhancements and formalise the changes required into an
action plan. As new risks are identified or presented, consideration will be given to the
modification of the program and strategies to execute upon the program.
Before new products or services are launched, the business must review the product or service for
potential ML/TF/PF risks and ensure appropriate controls are implemented if such risks exist.
The UK MLRO
● Conducts and documents a risk assessment and analysis for ML, TF and PF risks related to
the products and services offered. A risk assessment and analysis is required no less than
annually and may be conducted more frequently, if required.
● Presents results of the risk assessment to the Board of Directors annually.
● Incorporates recommendations from the results of the risk assessment into this Financial
Crime Program and, where applicable, controls into the relevant products or services.
6. Know your Customer
Regulation 27 & 28 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 requires firms to determine the level
of CDD to be applied on a risk based approach. SEL uses a formal process to obtain information
on its customers and/or controlling individuals in its customers' businesses to reasonably assess
their identity and legitimacy, as required by law and in line with SELʼs risk based approach. SEL
onboards the following entity types, sole traders, limited companies, partnerships and limited
partnerships.
It should be noted that SEL does not onboard high risk business such as:
● Firms that have, bearer shares as part of the ownership chain; or
● Firms with less transparent structures, with numerous layers.
The nature of SELʼs current businesses is that customers interact through cards and bank accounts
has significantly less risk than some other financial services businesses. The due diligence checks
and processes are built into the onboarding process that a customer completes to sign up and use
the Square product and its services. The verification of a customerʼs identity is conducted at the
start of the business relationship through a third party tool for identification and verification (“IDVˮ)
purposes.
For managed payments, a risk profile is assigned to the customer at onboarding to allow for
greater attention to certain risk levels to ensure appropriate due diligence is carried out. Included
in this risk profile for managed payments is an ‘expected activityʼ field which must be completed by
the applicant. This data can be used for comparison by an analyst to review at a later date if the
customer indicates a higher risk profile through transaction monitoring,PEPscreening, or the CRR
model.

If adverse information is found on an individual or business during the onboarding process, the
Compliance Team reviews all information available and makes a determination as to whether such
information precludes an approval, based on SELʼs Terms of Service and its Financial Crime
Program. Following the review, a decision to approve or decline the customer is made, along with

SEL maintains KYC processes and procedures in line with this Financial Crime Program.SELʼs

7. Acceptance Criteria for Loans
In addition to applying its standard KYC checks during onboarding, SEL has set specific
acceptance criteria when offering loans to ensure that the exposure to ML/TF/PF is kept to a
minimum. SEL takes the approach of only offering loans to businesses which demonstrate low risk
behaviours, such as:
● They have processed at least £10,000 or more in a 12 month period;
● Their card payments processed through the Square remain consistent and frequent;
● The customers who have shopped with them are a good mix of new and returning
customers; and
● They are showing signs of healthy and steady growth.
Due to the nature of SELʼs target customers, a business that does the opposite of this may have
increased chances of taking part in ML/TF/PF. The above would act as a preventive method to
stop, or at least make it more difficult for a purposely established shell company to sign up and use
loans as a means to launder the proceeds of crime.
8. Customer Risk Rating Model
The cornerstone of a strong AML compliance program is the adoption and implementation of
risk-based due diligence policies and procedures for all customers, particularly those that present
a higher risk for ML,TF and PF. In order to achieve this, SEL has designed and implemented a CRR
program that assesses a customer's profile and assigns risk rating and monitors the risk levels of
the customers.
The CRR program enables SEL to apply the appropriate risk-based controls to mitigate exposure to
ML, TF,, and financial crime. Improper assessment of a customerʼs risk can have a cascading
effect, creating deficiencies in multiple areas of internal controls and resulting in an overall
weakened AML compliance program.
9. Politically Exposed Persons
A PEPis defined in Regulation 3512a & 14 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 as
someone holding a prominent public position who is entrusted with prominent public functions
either in the UK or elsewhere in the world. This includes both domestic and non-domestic PEPs. It
also includes their family and known close associates.
Family includes:

● Spouse or partner of the person;
● Children of the person, their spouses or partners; and
● Parents of that person.
A known close associate is an individual:
● Known to have joint beneficial ownership of a legal entity or legal arrangement or any other
close business relations with a PEP; or
● Who has sole beneficial ownership of a legal entity or a legal arrangement which is known
to have been set up for the benefit of a PEP.
SEL is not precluded from establishing business relationships with PEPs, but it recognises that
PEPs potentially pose a higher ML and TF risk because individuals holding such positions may
misuse their power or influence for personal gain or advantage. PEPs may also use family or close
associates to hide funds or assets obtained through the abuse of their position or bribery and
corruption.
SEL is aligned with the FCA guidance of the definition of a PEP and who should be treated as a PEP
and regularly reviews updates published by the FCA on its guidance.

13. Enhanced Due Diligence
SEL has designed an EDD monitoring process as part of its KYC Program and inline with the
requirements detailed in Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. Merchants
identified that could potentially pose an ML/TF/PF or other financial crime risk undergo EDD,
meaning the merchantsʼ accounts are reviewed with additional scrutiny and frequency once the
risk is identified. Merchants are identified primarily through the CRR model.
An EDD review will be triggered if an account meets one or more of the below criteria or direction
is received from the MLRO/DMLRO
● Accounts with confirmed Law enforcement hits;
● Accounts with confirmed PEP/RCA (“Relative or Close Associateˮ) hits;
● Accounts with a high risk Merchant Category Code (“MCCˮ);
● Merchants identified through the CRR; or
● A merchant or transactions present a higher risk of ML/TF/PF.
The MLRO/DMLRO reserves the right to mandate EDD at any point during the customer
relationship, regardless of whether a trigger event has occurred. This discretionary authority
allows the MLRO/DMLRO to respond dynamically to emerging risks, intelligence or regulatory
requirements.
The analyst reviews all information available, as part of the EDD review and on completing their
review, escalates all EDD cases where enforcement hits or PEP hits are confirmed and all EDD
cases where suspicious activity has been detected to the MLRO/DMLRO for approval. The MLROʼs
decision to approve or decline the customer is then made, along with the documented rationale.
14. Monitoring Program
SEL monitors the activity of customers to identify any transactions, patterns, or behaviours which
appear to be potentially suspicious and related to ML/TF/PF inline with the requirements detailed in
Regulation 2811 and Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. SEL uses a
combination of analytic models, in-house front-end software applications, vendor supported
analytics, and manual analysis to review activity on an ongoing basis from the beginning to the end
of the business relationship.
The analytic models apply a set of curated algorithms to run against the transaction data set and
populate a set of review queues. The Compliance Team reviews the queues on a daily basis. In
addition, the Risk Management Team reviews possible fraud related activity via their independent
queues, with escalation to the Compliance Team. The analytic models employ a dynamic,
risk-based approach taking into account a number of factors unique to the merchantʼs business.
The Compliance Team reviews the algorithms using ongoing analysis of transaction data,
information from the risk assessment, and takes into account the type of product and service in
use.

The risks factor include, but are not limited to:
● A customer that repays a loan faster than expected;
● Any transaction that appears to have no apparent economic or visible lawful purpose;
● Any unusual patterns of transactions involving a merchant or merchant's customer that
have no apparent economic or visible lawful purpose;
● Any unusual patterns of transaction involving linked customers, assets, or information;
● The pattern of activity expected based on the MCC, time of day, and historical pattern of
activity of the account and similar merchants;
● Payment instrument diversity in the transaction mix for the customer;
● Multiple payments from the same card or large payments;
● A customer availing of a lending product fails to make the first payment or makes an initial
payment but no subsequent payments;
● Personal identification provided by the customer is the same as that submitted by other
persons opening an account or other customers;
● A customer that satisfies a loan obligation, and there is a second loan that is taken
immediately afterward;
● Discrepancies in the provided personal identifying information when compared against
external information sources used by the SEL;
● Personal identifying information provided is of a type commonly associated with fraudulent
activity as indicated by internal or third-party sources used by SEL; and
● External factors (e.g., business status, bankruptcy records, ccjʼs, etc).
The output from the analytic models are reviewed using a set of factors to determine whether the
activity is potentially suspicious. The baseline factors are a set of common red flag indicators
based on AML laws and best practices.
The factors include, but are not limited to:
● Customers who exhibit an unusual amount of concern regarding SELʼs KYCprocedures and
reporting requirements, particularly if they demonstrate reluctance to reveal their identity
or provide relevant documentation about their business or transactions;
● Merchants who engage in transactions inconsistent with their stated business type and
activity (for example: unsubstantiated velocity of transactions, irregular transaction
amounts, times, and locations);
● individuals who engage in inconsistent transaction patterns or activity (for example:
unsubstantiated velocity of transactions, irregular transaction amounts, times, and
locations);
● Customers who provide false, misleading, or substantially incorrect information;
● Repeated attempts where an individual is unable to match identification data points;
● Customers who have questionable backgrounds, or are the subject of negative news
reports and/or publicly available information indicating possible criminal, civil, or regulatory
enforcement or violations;
● Merchants who purport to be registered agents for a business but decline to provide
information about the principal and/or the entity in question;
● Merchants who have difficulty describing the nature of their business or lack general
knowledge of their industry;

● Customers who engage in transactions structured to evade amounts just below reportable
or other thresholds, if applicable;
● Customers who for no apparent reason have multiple accounts under a single name or
multiple names, or multiple accounts sharing the same identity and/or bank account;
● Merchants who sign up to use SELʼs services through an entity such as a trust, shell
company or Private Investment Company; and
● Customers with overly complex ownership structures, and/or hold multiple directorships in
an unrealistic amount of companies.
In addition to red flag indicators, the Compliance Analyst evaluating the activity associated with a
given Customerʼs account will use a set of positive and negative indicators as part of the
decision-making process, on a risk based approach.
Ongoing monitoring of SELʼs Square customers is conducted to manage and mitigate risks that
could arise during the business relationship, for example where:
● The customer is a PEP or a family member or close associate of a PEP;
● A single transaction or multiple transactions occur where the activity relates to a high risk
jurisdiction or is complex, unusual, or appears to have no economic or legal purpose;
● There is a higher risk of ML, TF or PF due to other factors (e.g., business type according to
MCC;
● False or misleading information is identified during a review; or
● False or misleading information is identified through the CRR model.
The output of this ongoing monitoring may require the enEDD process to be followed, the details of
which are contained in the EDD process document. The process is in line with Part 3, Chapter 2 of
the Money Laundering Regulations 2017.
SEL acknowledges that certain types of businesses are more likely to be associated with ML.
Accordingly, all businesses that are classified in one of the following categories will receive
increased scrutiny from the Compliance Team:
● Individual use;
● Political organisation;
● Charitable organisations; and
● Professional services
Finally, risk-based models are used to evaluate all MCCs for expected account behaviour. The risk
based process allows SEL to prioritise attention on the accounts that exhibit possible higher levels
of risk when account behaviour does not match what is expected. SEL maintains transaction
monitoring processes/procedures in line with this Financial Crime Program.
It is important to ensure that SEL keeps abreast of new typologies and changes to typologies (e.g.
new corridors of activity, new thresholds etc.). The Compliance teams ensure that the transaction
monitoring program reflects the risk in the activities of its customers.
In addition, SEL reflects the nature of the regulated area in which it operates, in this case the
National Risk Assessments, updates from regulators and law enforcement and trade organisations

also inform the risk based approach of SEL. The teams carrying out transaction monitoring must

15. Reporting Suspicious Activity
The purpose of this section is to define the responsibilities and legal obligations for identifying and
reporting of suspicious activity in compliance with;
● POCA;
● TACT 2000;
● Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Information on the Payer)
Regulations 2017;
● FCA Handbook SYSC 6.3.7; And
● NCA SARs Regime guidance
s330 POCA requires SEL to report potentially suspicious transactions or activity by completing a
suspicious activity or transaction report, within the relevant timeframe, and to collect and maintain
supporting documentation in accordance with the record retention requirements.
All SEL employees are required to remain alert to any behaviour or transactional activity that may
give rise to suspicion and must immediately refer the activity to the Compliance Team. This
includes inconsistencies between the merchants declared business activities and the actual
transactional patterns, sudden or unexplained changes in processing volumes, frequent or
excessive refunds/chargebacks or links to jurisdictions, individuals or entities that are subject to
sanctions or adverse media.
Where such indicators arise, staff must promptly submit an Internal SAR to the MLRO/DMLRO as
soon as practicable. This report must be completed using the SEL designated internal escalation
SAR process.
The MLRO/DMLRO upon receipt of such an internal report will evaluate whether the suspicion
meets the legal threshold for filing a SAR with the UK Financial Intelligence Unit (“UKFIUˮ), which
forms part of the NCA.
Failure to comply with these reporting obligations may constitute a criminal offence, including
under Section 330 of POCA, which pertains to the failure to disclose.
Once this reporting has been completed by the employee, contractor, director, officer, or agent,
they have discharged their personal obligation in this respect and the MLRO/DMLRO is to
acknowledge receipt of such reporting. SEL maintains SAR processes and procedures in line with
this Financial Crime Program.
The MLRO/DMLRO is responsible for maintaining a secure and auditable log of all internal reports,
external SARs (submissions to the NCA and related documentation. This includes any
communications with NCA, law enforcement agencies or regulators. Records must be retained for
a minimum period of five years following the end of the business relationship or the date of the

report, whichever is later. These records must be readily available for inspection by the FCA or
other competent authorities upon request.

15.3 Confidentially & Tipping Off
According to section 333A POCA Section, and section 21D of the TACT 2000 and POCA 2002
Amendment) Regulations 2007, SEL can commit a tipping-off offence if it does anything that may
tip off another person that a suspicion has been raised, discloses to a third person that a SAR has
been filed, or discloses that an investigation into an offence is being contemplated or carried out if
SELʼs disclosure is likely to prejudice any criminal investigation. Accordingly, all SARs must be kept
confidential. Confidential information also includes any documentation stating that a SAR was or
was not filed and the basis on which the decision was made.
All SAR contemplation, resulting in filing to the NCA or a no-SAR decision SAR recorded internally
only), must not be disclosed or even hinted to the Customer as well as all other subjects of the
SAR, or anyone within SEL, SELʼs parent company, or any of its affiliated companies that does not
have a need to know of such actions.
SEL holds SARs and any supporting documentation in a confidential folder. SEL does not inform
anyone outside of a law enforcement or regulatory agency about the details of a SAR. In addition,
the details of a SAR filings and copies of supporting documentation are segregated from other SEL
records to avoid disclosing SAR details.
15.4 Law Enforcement & Regulatory Bodies
SEL responds to any law enforcement, regulatory bodies, government agency, and other financial
institution requests for documentation on Customers by searching its records to determine
whether it has engaged in any transaction with the individual or entity named in the request. If a
match is found, it is reported to the appropriate authority. If the search does not uncover a match,
then SEL will reply in accordance with local laws and regulations.
SEL does not disclose the fact that there has been a request to any Customers, except to the
extent necessary to comply with the information request from a law enforcement or regulatory
body.

SELʼs Compliance function does not use information provided for any purpose other than 1 to
report to appropriate law enforcement or government agency; 2 to determine whether to
establish or maintain a relationship; or 3 to assist SEL in complying with any information sharing
regulatory requirements.
The MLRO regularly reviews the content of such reports to:
● Determine whether SARS are warranted to be submitted to the NCA;
● Consider whether the monitoring program for SEL needs refinement, in the light of the
intelligence from these requests; and
● Report to the Board of Directors and senior management on the nature of such activity
occurring utilising SELʼs infrastructure.
16. Data protection
SEL gathers all required Customer personal information as applicable to the products and services
utilised by the Customer. The personal information gathered for the KYC requirements are used
only for that purpose and consent to use this data is obtained from the Customer in a formal
process when the Customer signs up for the specific product or service. SEL complies with
personal identification requirements as required by in-country laws where they are stricter than
SELʼs own policy.
The following information is required and obtained:
● For Managed Payment Customers; Full Legal Name, Residential Address and Date of Birth
of an Individual, Account Holder, Beneficial Owner, as applicable;
● Full Legal Name of Directors, as applicable;
● Individual/Account Holder Phone Number, as applicable;
● Individual/Account Holder Email Address, as applicable;
● Customerʼs Bank Account or Card information, as applicable;
● Company Structure and Ownership Structure;
● Legal and Trading business Addresses, as applicable; and
● Due Diligence verification on data and information provided per the Third Party Vendors.
17. Quality Control
A quality control process exists as a process to review the quality of investigative research,
actions, documentation, and the associated outcomes. It reviews actions taken by the Transaction
Monitoring team for consistency with established procedures and to provide reporting for
management and senior leadership teams and the MLRO on the results of the reviews.
The results identify opportunities for training and development, as well as continuous improvement
for the local compliance team and the Financial Crime Program overall. The process includes
discussing with the analyst any concerns noted during the quality assurance review, re-training,
and providing corrective actions for issues noted, as applicable.

18. Outsourcing Arrangements
18.1 Governance
SEL acknowledges that the majority of AML/CTF/CPF and screening operational activity is
outsourced to Block and SIL. SEL maintains oversight of these activities through the use of robust
SLAs, regular engagement, and oversight through service standards reviews and assurance testing
conducted by the Compliance team and the monitoring and testing group functions.
SEL has in place an outsourcing framework to ensure a structured oversight. The Program is
established in line with the European Banking Authority (“EBAˮ) and it will also be mindful of any
outsourcing guidelines issued by the FCA in the development of and implementation of its
outsourcing program.
SELʼs Outsourcing Policy outlines roles and responsibilities and arrangements for oversight of any
outsourced service.The Board of Directors retains the ultimate responsibility for effective
oversight of outsourcing.
SEL keeps records of all the outsourcing arrangements including the intra-group, third parties, and
critical and non-critical functions. SEL performs the same level of due diligence on intra-group
outsourcing arrangements and third party outsourcing arrangements.
SEL keeps an up to date Outsourcing Register and utilises the Outsourcing Oversight Committee
(“OCCˮ) to monitor and oversee its outsourcing arrangements.
18.2 Enhanced Risk Management and Controls
Risk assessments are conducted before entering an outsourcing arrangement. SEL has an OCC
deliberating on a monthly basis, and considerations of outsourcing arrangements are evidenced by
records of discussions and decisions;
AML functions outsourced intra-group, including KYC, IDV, Customer Due Diligence, Transaction
Monitoring, Sanctions Screening, Suspicious Activity Investigation and Customer Complaints, are
overseen by the UK Head of Compliance/MLRO.

SELʼs Financial Crime Program is assessed for effectiveness through regulatory examinations,

internal control experience, an outside auditor, or some other independent professional.

20. Compliance
20.1 Roles and Responsibilities
● Money Laundering Reporting Officer: SELʼs Board has appointed a MLRO, who leads the
compliance effort and has overall responsibility for the execution of the Financial Crime
Program in the UK. The MLRO has the full authorisation and approval from the Board of
Directors and senior management of SEL to act independently of them to fulfil the role. The
MLRO has sufficient and appropriate AML/CTF/CPF knowledge and expertise. The MLRO
has autonomy and has the ability to escalate matters to the Board if required.
The MLRO is principally responsible for:
○ Receiving reports of potentially suspicious activity from any employee of, or officer
in, SEL or from external sources such as law enforcement and considering all alerts
and evaluating whether there is, (or seems to be), any evidence of ML, TF, PF or
other financial crime;
○ Reporting any suspicious activity or transaction to the relevant local government or
law enforcement agency by completing and submitting a suspicious activity report
(“SARˮ);
○ Ensuring that there are the appropriate systems and processes in place to respond
fully and rapidly to enquiries by law enforcement agencies including the Serious
Fraud Office (“SFOˮ), National Crime Agency (“NCAˮ), and the FCA;
○ Ensuring AML procedures and controls are in place and operating efficiently;
○ Periodically receiving, reviewing, and challenging management information where
appropriate;
○ Implementing a program to monitor any situations of higher risk of ML, TF or PF.
○ Ensuring that a risk assessment of ML, TF, PF or other financial crime is carried out
on at least an annual basis and presented to Senior Management and the Board;.
○ Confirming adequacy of procedures for record keeping in relation to financial
crime;
○ Training employees and the Board of Directors on the prevention of ML, TF and PF;
○ Designating appropriate responsibilities and actions to other key individuals and
team members, who possess the requisite skills and knowledge to execute such
tasks;
○ The approval of outsourcing compliance activity, to include outsourcing
intra-group, in line with global and local outsourcing policies and applicable
regulatory requirements;
○ Informing in a regular and timely manner senior management and members of the
Board of Directors on material compliance matters, key program updates, and key

risk indicators and metrics through the quarterly updates to the Board and annual
MLRO reports; and
○ Producing an MLRO Report annually, taking guidance from the Joint Money
Laundering Steering Group (“JMLSGˮ).
● Three Lines of Defence: SEL has adopted the ‘Three lines of defence modelʼ that ensures
the following:
○ There is adequate coordination between the first line business/operational teams
and control functions such as Risk, Compliance, and Internal Audit, to ensure
effective oversight and effective coordination of resources to manage overlap in
areas of review;
○ Second and third line work plans are risk-based with all risks and controls,
including AML/CTF/CPF risks and controls, reviewed on a regular basis
commensurate with the risk;
○ The MLRO is involved in the planning of the scheduled second line of defence
reviews and in the closing of the findings arising from second line and third line of
defence reviews of the Financial Crime Program;
○ All issues are effectively tracked and managed to resolution;
○ Risk, Compliance, and Internal Audit teams are independent and resourced with
Staff knowledgeable of AML/CTF/CPF.
● Compliance Operations Teams Block, Inc. & AML RightSource): These teams provide
first line processing of alerts for:
○ Transaction Monitoring;
○ Sanctions and PEP screening and Adverse Media;
○ Know Your Customer (“KYCˮ), Customer Risk Rating (“CRRˮ), EDD; and
○ SAR reporting.
Note: All decisioning on these processes is ultimately the responsibility of the MLRO or
their designee)
● Project Management, Independent Testing and Monitoring, Internal Audit Regulatory
Counsel, Product Compliance, Risk: These teams provide support to the MLRO in carrying
out various functions associated with the role, such as:
○ Designing, implementing, and maintaining system-based compliance controls;
○ Carrying out independent testing and monitoring and audits;
○ Managing the timely delivery of key projects;
○ Providing regulatory and technical advice and counsel;and
○ Investigating and preventing fraud, including referral to the Operations teams for
SAR filing.
● Head of Governance, Compliance Block This individual provides oversight of key
support functions related to compliance governance such as:
○ Policies, compliance training, and board and management reporting;
○ Testing and monitoring of compliance controls across all SEL products;
○ Risk assessment and analysis, change management, audit and exam management,
and issue management;

○ Program-and policy-level work, as well as understanding compliance risk with
regard to our partners; and
○ Due diligence and monitoring programs for both upstream partners, such as the
payment networks and partner banks, and our downstream partners, such as third
party vendors.
20.2 Training
New Hire Training
At a company-wide level, all new employees receive AML/CTF/CPF training, through an online based
interactive training course. The online based interactive training has a comprehensive assessment at
the end of the course. The AML/CTF/CPF training course is updated at a minimum annually.
Refresher Training
All employees are required to take refresher training on an annual basis. The AML/CTF/CPF
training is designed to enable the employees to understand:
● What activities amount to ML, TF and PF and how they should be reported;
● Key elements of the AML/CTF/CPF laws including coverage of:
○ developing and maintaining a Financial Crime Program and the key features of
such;
○ SELʼs KYC obligations;
○ SELʼs reporting obligations; and
○ record keeping and retention obligations;
● The type of ML/TF/PF risks that we might face and how employees are able to spot these
risks;
● The consequences of non-compliance with the AML/CTF/CPF laws, on SEL and its
employees; and
● The effect on society of the failure to implement good Financial Crime Programs.
Targeted AML/CTF/CPF
SEL provides more in-depth AML training programs to:
● Local Customer Success teams
● Sales and Account Executive team
● Board of Directors, in person/via video conference (on annual basis)
● Compliance personnel whereby a training program is tailored to their compliance role and
regulatory issues and risk specific to their region. Training may include:
○ Webinars provided by established AML organisations such as Association of
Certified Anti-Money Laundering Specialists (“ACAMSˮ) or the International
Compliance Association (“ICA");
○ government entities;
○ Distribution of written materials;
○ AML conferences;
○ Department meetings; or
○ Other internally developed training sessions.

Additionally, processes are thoroughly documented enabling new employees to quickly and
effectively learn and perform their core job functions.

Appendix 1 SEL Products and Services
SEL MANAGED PAYMENTS POS SYSTEMS
Point of Sale ("POS") system is the software application (app) that is used to provide the technical
interface between the hardware Square Reader, Stand, Terminal, Register) and the Merchant's
acquiring banking infrastructure. The app is free to download. SEL's hardware and software can be
integrated with third-party business applications such as accounting software website builder
apps.
Square Managed Payments
A Square Reader, a Square Stand, a Square Terminal, a Square Register and the Square Managed
Payments POS system, collectively make up the payment solution referred to as Square Managed
Payments. Square Managed Payments reads debit, credit, prepaid, and gift card data and enables
a Merchant to:
● accept payments using their iPhone/iPad/Android mobile device, Square Terminal or
Square Register;
● send a digital receipt to their customer (or a printed receipt if compatible hardware is
installed) for payment of goods and services the Merchant offers; and
● Sellers will be able to tap to pay on Android by using their Android device as a POS for
contactless payments within the authorised amount.
Square Web POS
This is a virtual terminal solution, allowing Merchants to process payments from their browser, on
their in store computer or laptop, taking payments over the phone, in person, or via text message.
All-in-one POS system to manage your front of house and back of house. Includes table maps,
order managers and live sales tracking.
Square Invoice
Allows Merchants to send an Invoice to customers and accept online payments.
Customer Directory
It is a contact management software that allows the Merchant to store vital business information on
the Square Managed Payments. It creates a Customer Directory to send emails and make
appointments. It allows the Merchant to understand their Customer by seeing and researching their
spending habits.
Employee Management
This facility allows Merchants to manage access for employees using the Square Managed
Payments. It can limit what information an employee can see, track when the employees log in and
out of the system, and allows different access levels/roles.
Inventory Management
It allows the possibility to import and export thousands of products quickly and receive low or out
of stock alerts with multi-location inventory management

Location Management
Run multiple locations from the one account from the Square Managed Payments dashboard. It
allows the facility to adjust menu items, prices, and employees per location.
Customer Feedback
The customer feedback system allows people to leave feedback directly through their digital
receipt and lets the Merchants see how they are doing in real time.
Card on File
It allows the possibility to save payment information to a customer profile. This helps the
Merchants to store payment details for their regular, loyal Customers, in a secure and reliable
manner.
Square Loans
SEL offers unregulated credit to incorporated entities, individuals and partnerships that are
“relevant recipients of creditˮ where it is at a value exceeding £25,000. SEL has developed
expertise across the entire “life of a loanˮ- risk assessment, loan sizing, product and language
simplicity, loan servicing, and collections- and have applied these capabilities to a breadth of
credit products. The goal is to empower underserved customers via contextual credit options that
are fair and simple to use. Loan products are offered to customers that meet SELʼs credit and
affordability criteria mentioned in its credit policy.
Square APIs
This is SEL's eCommerce application programming interface product which allows Merchants with
an online presence to accept debit, credit, prepaid and gift card payments via several APIs.
Square Point of Sale API
This allows companies to link their own custom app to the Square Point of Sale app and then take
card-present payments via Square.
Square Terminal API
This is Squareʼs solution to integrate payments accepted through Square Terminal with custom
solutions on the Merchantʼs side.
Instant Deposits
This feature allows Square Merchants to initiate deposits to their bank account at any point in time
throughout the day, including weekends and holidays.
Square Loyalty Programme
This feature allows Square Merchants to have a customisable Loyalty programme that is integrated
with their POS system.
Square Self Serve Ordering
This feature allows offering digital menus for customers to place orders and pay at their tables or
kiosks using QR codes.
Square Photo Studio

This allows the Square Merchants to have the ability to take pictures and upload it to an online
platform.
Square Online Features
This feature allows Square Merchants to create an Online store, restaurant ordering page,
Instagram shopping page or professional ecommerce website with built in order fulfilment tools.
The Square Merchant can also generate a link or button that can be clicked on by a buyer to
purchase any product or service and does not require a website.
Square Web Payments SDK
The Square Web Payments SDK allows users to build highly customisable checkout pages for
online websites and accept online payments through cards or digital wallets.
